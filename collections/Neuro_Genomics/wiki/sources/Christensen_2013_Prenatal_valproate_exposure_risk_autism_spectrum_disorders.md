# Prenatal valproate exposure and risk of autism spectrum disorders and childhood autism.

## Abstract

**IMPORTANCE:** Valproate is used for the treatment of epilepsy and other neuropsychological disorders and may be the only treatment option for women of childbearing potential. However, prenatal exposure to valproate may increase the risk of autism.

**OBJECTIVE:** To determine whether prenatal exposure to valproate is associated with an increased risk of autism in offspring.

**DESIGN, SETTING, AND PARTICIPANTS:** Population-based study of all children born alive in Denmark from 1996 to 2006. National registers were used to identify children exposed to valproate during pregnancy and diagnosed with autism spectrum disorders (childhood autism [autistic disorder], Asperger syndrome, atypical autism, and other or unspecified pervasive developmental disorders). We analyzed the risks associated with all autism spectrum disorders as well as childhood autism. Data were analyzed by Cox regression adjusting for potential confounders (maternal age at conception, paternal age at conception, parental psychiatric history, gestational age, birth weight, sex, congenital malformations, and parity). Children were followed up from birth until the day of autism spectrum disorder diagnosis, death, emigration, or December 31, 2010, whichever came first.

**MAIN OUTCOMES AND MEASURES:** Absolute risk (cumulative incidence) and the hazard ratio (HR) of autism spectrum disorder and childhood autism in children after exposure to valproate in pregnancy.

**RESULTS:** Of 655,615 children born from 1996 through 2006, 5437 were identified with autism spectrum disorder, including 2067 with childhood autism. The mean age of the children at end of follow-up was 8.84 years (range, 4-14; median, 8.85). The estimated absolute risk after 14 years of follow-up was 1.53% (95% CI, 1.47%-1.58%) for autism spectrum disorder and 0.48% (95% CI, 0.46%-0.51%) for childhood autism. Overall, the 508 children exposed to valproate had an absolute risk of 4.42% (95% CI, 2.59%-7.46%) for autism spectrum disorder (adjusted HR, 2.9 [95% CI, 1.7-4.9]) and an absolute risk of 2.50% (95% CI, 1.30%-4.81%) for childhood autism (adjusted HR, 5.2 [95% CI, 2.7-10.0]). When restricting the cohort to the 6584 children born to women with epilepsy, the absolute risk of autism spectrum disorder among 432 children exposed to valproate was 4.15% (95% CI, 2.20%-7.81%) (adjusted HR, 1.7 [95% CI, 0.9-3.2]), and the absolute risk of childhood autism was 2.95% (95% CI, 1.42%-6.11%) (adjusted HR, 2.9 [95% CI, 1.4-6.0]) vs 2.44% (95% CI, 1.88%-3.16%) for autism spectrum disorder and 1.02% (95% CI, 0.70%-1.49%) for childhood autism among 6152 children not exposed to valproate.

**CONCLUSIONS AND RELEVANCE:** Maternal use of valproate during pregnancy was associated with a significantly increased risk of autism spectrum disorder and childhood autism in the offspring, even after adjusting for maternal epilepsy. For women of childbearing potential who use antiepileptic medications, these findings must be balanced against the treatment benefits for women who require valproate for epilepsy control.

## Key findings

- **Cohort scale.** Population-based cohort of 655,615 Danish children born 1996–2006 (born to 428,407 mothers; 26,418 multiple births, 4.0%), followed to a mean age of 8.84 y (median 8.85; range 4–14). 5437 had ASD and 2067 had childhood autism. 2644 children were exposed to antiepileptic drugs (AEDs) in utero, of which 508 were exposed to valproate.
- **Headline effect.** Any maternal valproate use in pregnancy raised the adjusted HR to **2.9 (95% CI 1.7–4.9) for ASD** and **5.2 (95% CI 2.7–10.0) for childhood autism**; absolute 14-y risks rose to 4.42% (ASD) and 2.50% (childhood autism) vs background 1.53% and 0.48%. Adjustment was for maternal/paternal age, parental psychiatric history, gestational age, birth weight, sex, congenital malformations, and parity.
- **Effect persists after restricting to mothers with epilepsy** (the key confounding-by-indication test): among 6584 children of women with epilepsy, valproate-exposed (n=432) adjusted HR 1.7 (95% CI 0.9–3.2, not significant) for ASD but **2.9 (95% CI 1.4–6.0) for childhood autism**; absolute risks 4.15% (ASD) and 2.95% (childhood autism) vs 2.44% and 1.02% in unexposed children of epileptic mothers. The attenuated, CI-spanning-1 ASD estimate within epilepsy indicates residual confounding by indication and/or limited power.
- **"Stopped before pregnancy" comparison (addresses confounding by indication directly).** Children of current valproate users had higher risk than children of former users who stopped ≥30 d before conception: adjusted HR 2.2 (95% CI 1.02–4.9) for ASD and 5.6 (95% CI 1.7–18.1) for childhood autism — supporting an effect of the drug exposure itself rather than the underlying illness.
- **Drug specificity.** Other AED monotherapies showed no significant ASD/childhood-autism association: oxcarbazepine (ASD HR 2.1, 95% CI 0.96–4.6), lamotrigine (1.7, 0.8–3.5), carbamazepine (1.0, 0.4–2.8), clonazepam (0.7, 0.2–2.9). Valproate is a fatty-acid derivative chemically distinct from the others, which the authors offer as a possible explanation.
- **Monotherapy vs polytherapy.** Valproate monotherapy (n=388): adjusted HR 3.0 (1.7–5.4) ASD, 4.9 (2.3–10.3) childhood autism. Valproate polytherapy (n=120): ASD HR 1.9 (0.5–7.7, NS), childhood autism 5.2 (1.3–21.3).
- **Dose.** Risk seen at both high (>750 mg/d, n=183: ASD HR 2.5 [1.03–6.1]) and low (≤750 mg/d, n=325: ASD HR 3.2 [1.7–6.2]) doses; no clear monotonic dose-response, but actual ingested dose was not available.
- **Timing of exposure.** First-trimester exposure (n=441): ASD HR 2.9 (1.6–5.1), childhood autism 4.7 (2.2–9.9). Later-only exposure (n=67): ASD HR 3.1 (0.8–12.2), childhood autism 8.3 (2.1–32.4). No clear early-vs-late difference; later-only group small. Varying the pre-pregnancy exposure window (0/90/180 d before) changed ASD HRs only modestly (3.0 / 2.7 / 2.3).
- **Robust to malformations.** Excluding the 25,619 children with congenital malformations, valproate-exposed (n=457) retained adjusted HR 2.8 (1.5–5.1) ASD and 4.2 (1.9–9.3) childhood autism — the autism signal is not merely a byproduct of teratogenic malformation.
- **Sex ratio.** Among valproate-exposed ASD cases the boy:girl ratio (adjusted for time to diagnosis) was 2.4 (95% CI 0.8–7.7) vs 4.4 (4.1–4.7) in unexposed (P=.32); for childhood autism 1.2 (0.3–4.4) vs 4.6 (4.1–5.1) (P=.04), hinting valproate exposure may attenuate the usual male preponderance, though exposed case counts are very small.
- **Background prevalence concordance.** The 14-y ASD prevalence estimate of 1.53% is consistent with US estimates (~1.1% parent-reported; ~1% in 8-y-olds, ADDM Network), supporting register validity.

## Methods

- **Design:** Nationwide population-based register cohort study (Denmark), Cox proportional-hazards regression with child's age as the time scale and birth-year strata to absorb the secular rise in ASD diagnosis. Robust (Huber-White) variance to handle non-independence of siblings. Absolute risk in exposed estimated by multiplying Kaplan-Meier unexposed cumulative incidence by the adjusted HR (valid because ASD prevalence <10%).
- **Data sources:** Danish Civil Registration System (cohort assembly via unique personal ID); Danish Prescription Register (all filled prescriptions since 1996-01-01; AEDs require prescription in Denmark); Danish Psychiatric Central Register (ASD outcomes via ICD-10 F84.0/F84.1/F84.5/F84.8/F84.9; childhood autism = F84.0; parental psychiatric history); Danish Birth Register (gestational age, birth weight, parity); National Hospital Register (congenital malformations Q00–Q99 except Q53/Q65; parental epilepsy via ICD-8 345, ICD-10 G40/G41).
- **Exposure definition:** Any filled AED prescription (ATC N03A plus clobazam N05BA09) from 30 days before estimated conception to birth. Monotherapy vs polytherapy by number of distinct AEDs. Estimated mean daily dose from total filled amount / days in window; dichotomized at 50% of WHO Defined Daily Dose.
- **Outcome ascertainment quality:** 94% of register childhood-autism diagnoses met ICD-10 criteria on validation; epilepsy-diagnosis positive predictive value 81%.
- **Confounders adjusted:** maternal age, paternal age, parental psychiatric history, gestational age, birth weight, sex, congenital malformations, parity.
- **Sensitivity/secondary analyses:** stratification by maternal epilepsy; current-vs-former-user comparison; dose, trimester, polytherapy, individual non-valproate AEDs; exclusion of malformations; varied pre-pregnancy exposure windows; sex-ratio comparison. Authors pre-specified the primary outcome and flagged the secondary analyses as exploratory (no multiple-comparison correction). Analyses in Stata 12; <3% loss to follow-up. Deidentified data, informed-consent requirement waived.

## Relevance to the brain-organoid ASD review

- This is the **environmental / gene×environment epidemiology pillar** the review cites for *environmental convergence* onto ASD developmental vulnerability. It provides the strongest population-scale human evidence that a defined prenatal molecular insult — valproate (an HDAC inhibitor and known modifier of transcription/chromatin) — measurably raises ASD risk, complementing the genetic-convergence papers (Willsey 2013, Parikshak 2013).
- Motivates **valproate as a tractable, dose-controllable perturbation in brain organoids**: a programmable organoid platform can model "gene × VPA" interactions in human midfetal-like cortical tissue, testing whether environmental and genetic hits converge on the same deep/upper-layer cortical-projection-neuron programs that Willsey/Parikshak implicate.
- The mechanistic hooks the paper lists (HDAC inhibition, altered neuronal migration/differentiation, apoptosis, synaptic plasticity, folate metabolism) map directly onto the early-transcriptional-regulation and synaptic modules emphasized in Parikshak — supporting the review's argument that a single environmental agent can phenocopy/interact with ASD genetic architecture during a shared developmental window.
- The "even after adjusting for epilepsy" and "current vs former user" results give the review defensible language that the association reflects the **drug exposure during the developmental window**, not solely the maternal indication — the kind of causal-leaning human anchor an organoid modeling argument needs.

## Open questions / limitations

- **Confounding by indication not fully excluded.** Within mothers with epilepsy the ASD HR (1.7) lost significance (95% CI 0.9–3.2); the authors attribute residual signal to childhood autism but acknowledge possible confounding by maternal epilepsy or gene×valproate interaction.
- **Exposure misclassification:** filled prescriptions ≠ ingestion; trimester assigned by fill date, not actual intake; in-hospital AED use not captured (would bias toward the null). Actual valproate dose and within-pregnancy dose changes unknown, weakening dose-response interpretation.
- **No correction for multiple comparisons;** all stratified/secondary analyses are explicitly exploratory with small exposed-case counts (often <10, sometimes <5), giving wide CIs (e.g., later-only childhood autism HR 8.3, CI 2.1–32.4).
- **Surveillance/ascertainment bias possible:** valproate-exposed children may be examined more carefully (known adverse effects), which would inflate the apparent effect.
- **Unmeasured confounders:** no data on alcohol use in pregnancy (only registered alcohol-abuse diagnoses adjusted), folic-acid use, smoking, or other concomitant medications; residual confounding by unrecorded parental psychiatric illness cannot be excluded. Periconceptional folic acid (cited as ~39% risk reduction for childhood autism) was unmeasured but, if anything, valproate users take more folate, biasing toward underestimation.
- **Generalizability:** single high-income national health system with comprehensive registries; absolute ASD risk remained <5% even when exposed, which the authors stress for clinical counseling.
- **No mechanism tested.** This is an epidemiological association; biological mode of valproate action on the developing human brain is inferred from animal/in vitro literature, not measured here — precisely the gap an organoid model is positioned to address.
