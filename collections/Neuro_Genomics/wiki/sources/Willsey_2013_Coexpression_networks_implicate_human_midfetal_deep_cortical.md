# Coexpression networks implicate human midfetal deep cortical projection neurons in the pathogenesis of autism.

## Source

- Authors: Willsey AJ, Sanders SJ, Li M, Dong S, Tebbenkamp AT, Muhle RA, Reilly SK, Lin L et al.
- Journal: Cell (2013)
- DOI: [10.1016/j.cell.2013.10.020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2013.10.020)
- PMID: [24267886](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24267886/)
- Added via: manuscript_brain_organoid_v6 reference ingest

## Abstract

Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is a complex developmental syndrome of unknown etiology. Recent studies employing exome- and genome-wide sequencing have identified nine high-confidence ASD (hcASD) genes. Working from the hypothesis that ASD-associated mutations in these biologically pleiotropic genes will disrupt intersecting developmental processes to contribute to a common phenotype, we have attempted to identify time periods, brain regions, and cell types in which these genes converge. We have constructed coexpression networks based on the hcASD "seed" genes, leveraging a rich expression data set encompassing multiple human brain regions across human development and into adulthood. By assessing enrichment of an independent set of probable ASD (pASD) genes, derived from the same sequencing studies, we demonstrate a key point of convergence in midfetal layer 5/6 cortical projection neurons. This approach informs when, where, and in what cell types mutations in these specific genes may be productively studied to clarify ASD pathophysiology.

## Key findings

- **Spatiotemporal convergence on midfetal prefrontal/motor-somatosensory cortex (PFC-MSC).** Seeding coexpression networks with only 9 high-confidence ASD (hcASD) genes and testing enrichment of an independent 122-gene probable-ASD (pASD) set across 52 networks (13 temporal windows × 4 anatomical clusters), pASD enrichment localized to PFC-MSC at periods 3–5 (10–19 PCW; hypergeometric p = 9.9×10⁻⁶) and periods 4–6 (13–24 PCW; p = 1.2×10⁻³), with the strongest signal at period 3–5.
- **Survives stringent permutation.** Permutation testing (100,000 iterations, selecting pseudo-hcASD seeds weighted by gene size and GC content) retained significance for PFC-MSC period 3–5 (p = 0.003) and period 4–6 (p = 0.05) and for an MD-CBC (thalamus-cerebellum) network at period 8–10 (p = 0.04); the V1C-STC period 7–9 network dropped out (p = 0.8). The two midfetal PFC-MSC networks were the focus.
- **Nine hcASD seed genes** (de novo LoF count in parentheses): ANK2 (2, novel in this study), CHD8 (3), CUL3 (2), DYRK1A (3), GRIN2B (3), KATNAL2 (2), POGZ (2), SCN2A (3), TBR1 (2). Defined from 1043 ASD families (599 quartets + 444 trios) yielding 144 de novo LoF mutations; in SSC quartets, probands carried 75 de novo LoF in 72 probands vs 34 in 32 sibling controls (OR 2.21, 95% CI 1.45–3.36, p = 5×10⁻⁵).
- **Statistical gene-class thresholds.** ≥3 de novo LoF in one gene → ASD gene at FDR q = 0.0002 (>99.9% true); ≥2 LoF → hcASD (q = 0.02; 97.8% true); single LoF → pASD (q = 0.45; ~54.7% chance of being a true ASD gene). 122 pASD genes identified.
- **Networks capture true ASD biology.** pASD genes inside the midfetal networks had significantly lower TADA gene-association p values than expected (period 3–5 p = 6.0×10⁻⁵; period 4–6 p = 2.7×10⁻⁴; TADA integrates de novo + inherited + case-control [935 cases / 870 controls, ARRA Autism Sequencing Consortium]). Networks were also more internally connected than chance (period 3–5 p = 0.03; 4–6 p = 0.02).
- **Layer resolution → inner cortical plate (CPi).** Recreating networks within 9 laser-microdissected human fetal cortical layers (BrainSpan LMD, 4 brains, periods 4–6 / 15–21 PCW), only the **inner cortical plate (CPi, = neocortical layers 5/6)** showed significantly increased connectivity, and only for the period 3–5 network (corrected p = 2.7×10⁻⁴); the period 4–6 network was not significantly connected in any single layer.
- **Mouse time-course confirms deep layers, early window.** In neocortical layer-specific mouse RNA-seq (P4–P180), only deep-layer (L5/L6) markers were enriched in the period 3–5 network (hypergeometric p < 0.04), and only at early time points P4–P10 (≈ human late-midfetal to late-fetal) — ruling out the alternative that the signal came from neurons en route to superficial layers.
- **Cell-type marker analysis → L5/6 glutamatergic cortical projection neurons (CPNs).** Using a 40-gene, 5-cell-type marker panel, both midfetal networks were enriched specifically for cortical glutamatergic projection neurons (period 3–5 p = 0.02; period 4–6 p = 0.01), and the contributing markers were specific to deep-layer (L5/L6) CPNs; superficial-layer (L1–L4) CPN markers were not enriched.
- **TBR1 is the most connected hcASD hub** in the strongest (period 3–5) network. Tbr1⁻/⁻ vs Tbr1⁺/⁺ P0 mouse cortex RNA-seq found 4 network genes differentially expressed (NR4A2 and SV2B down; FEZF2 and NEFM up), all previously validated TBR1 ChIP-seq targets — but removing TBR1 from the analysis did not change the spatiotemporal convergence.
- **Protein-level validation.** Immunostaining/in situ in human period 5–6 frontal cortex confirmed expression of 5 representative hcASD genes (TBR1, POGZ, CHD8, DYRK1A by IHC; SCN2A by ISH) in CPi projection neurons. TBR1 and POGZ colocalized with FOXP2 (deep-layer subcortical-projection marker, CPi-exclusive) and, in some CPi neurons, with SATB2 (corticocortical-projection marker) — implicating both deep CPN subtypes.
- **Expected to be one of several convergence points.** None of the classic syndromic ASD genes (FMR1, TSC1, TSC2, PTEN) fall in these networks; NRXN1 and NLGN4X appear in the postnatal MD-CBC network. Authors frame midfetal L5/6 as a strong but not exclusive convergence node, prioritizing specificity over sensitivity.

## Methods

- **Expression substrate:** BrainSpan exon-array transcriptome (Kang et al. 2011; GEO GSE25219; Affymetrix Human Exon 1.0 ST), 16 brain regions from 57 clinically unremarkable postmortem brains (31 M, 26 F), spanning 15 neurodevelopmental periods from 5.7 PCW to 82 y. Pairwise Pearson correlations between each of the 9 hcASD seeds and 16,947 genes; for each seed, the top 20 partners with |r| ≥ 0.7 were retained; edges drawn at |r| ≥ 0.7.
- **Network grid:** 13 sliding 3-period temporal windows × 4 spatial clusters from hierarchical clustering of fetal (period 3–7) transcriptomes — (1) V1C-STC, (2) PFC-MSC, (3) STR/HIP/AMY, (4) MD-CBC — yielding 52 spatiotemporal networks. Enrichment first by hypergeometric test, then by 100,000-iteration permutation correcting for de novo LoF mutability (gene size, GC content).
- **Independent validations layered on:** TADA gene-level association; human LMD laminar microarray (9 layers, periods 4–6); mouse neocortical layer RNA-seq time course (P4–P180, Dcdc2a-GFP reporter, S1 cortex; superficial/L4/deep zones); 40-gene cell-type marker enrichment; Tbr1 knockout P0 cortex RNA-seq (DEX: |log2FC| ≥ 0.5, adj p ≤ 0.05); IHC/ISH on 17–20 PCW human frontal cortex.
- **Exome data:** 1043 families (987 published + 56 new SSC quartets); de novo LoF = premature stop, canonical splice-site disruption, or frameshift indel; all new de novo variants confirmed by PCR/Sanger.

## Relevance to the brain-organoid ASD review

- One of the **two anchor papers grounding the review's "convergence on a developmental vulnerability stage" argument.** It pinpoints *when* (human midfetal, ≈10–24 PCW), *where* (prefrontal + primary motor-somatosensory cortex), and *in what cell type* (deep-layer L5/6 glutamatergic cortical projection neurons, the inner cortical plate) genetically heterogeneous ASD risk converges.
- Directly motivates **organoid design specs**: a "programmable" brain-organoid platform for proactive ASD genetics should aim to recapitulate midfetal deep-layer cortical projection neuron identity (TBR1⁺/FEZF2⁺/FOXP2⁺ CPi neurons) within the implicated developmental window, so that mutations in hcASD genes can be studied in the cellular context where their effects are predicted to matter.
- Provides a **prioritized gene/marker set** for organoid perturbation and readout — the 9 hcASD seeds (esp. the TBR1 regulatory hub and its targets FEZF2/NR4A2/SV2B/NEFM) and the deep-CPN markers (FOXP2, SATB2) — supplying concrete molecular handles for the review's argument that heterogeneous mutations funnel into a tractable shared mechanism.
- Pairs with **Parikshak 2013** (same Cell issue): Willsey prioritizes specificity from 9 high-confidence seeds and lands on L5/6 + midfetal; Parikshak, using larger gene sets and an alternative network approach, also implicates CPNs and midfetal CPi but additionally highlights upper layers (L2/3). The review can present these as convergent-yet-complementary evidence that ASD risk concentrates in midfetal cortical projection-neuron development — the developmental stage an organoid model must capture. The two papers cross-cite each other explicitly.

## Open questions / limitations

- **Only a small fraction of ASD risk genes examined** (9 seeds; ~1000+ genes estimated to contribute). Authors explicitly expect additional, undetected points of spatial/temporal convergence; this study favored specificity over sensitivity.
- **Syndromic genes absent.** FMR1, TSC1, TSC2, PTEN do not appear in the highlighted networks, so the midfetal-L5/6 node does not encompass all ASD biology; e.g., postnatal thalamus-cerebellum (MD-CBC) signal and the NRXN1/NLGN4X network suggest other loci/regions matter.
- **Period 4–6 network not layer-resolvable** to a single layer, and human LMD data covered only a narrow developmental interval (periods 4–6), so deep-layer attribution leans on cross-species (mouse) extrapolation.
- **Correlation-based networks** can inflate apparent enrichment (addressed via permutation, but an inherent caveat); convergence reflects coexpression/co-regulation, not proven causal mechanism in the disease.
- **Postmortem bulk/exon-array expression** (Kang 2011) predates single-cell resolution; cell-type attribution is inferential via marker panels and LMD, not direct single-cell measurement.
- **Mechanism within CPi neurons unspecified.** The paper localizes convergence but does not establish what shared functional perturbation in midfetal L5/6 CPNs produces ASD — the gap an experimentally manipulable human organoid system is positioned to fill.
