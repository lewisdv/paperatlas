## Abstract

**PROBLEM/CONDITION:** Autism spectrum disorder (ASD).

**PERIOD COVERED:** 2022.

**DESCRIPTION OF SYSTEM:** The Autism and Developmental Disabilities Monitoring Network is an active surveillance program that estimates prevalence and characteristics of ASD and monitors timing of ASD identification among children aged 4 and 8 years. In 2022, a total of 16 sites (located in Arizona, Arkansas, California, Georgia, Indiana, Maryland, Minnesota, Missouri, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Puerto Rico, Tennessee, Texas [two sites: Austin and Laredo], Utah, and Wisconsin) conducted surveillance for ASD among children aged 4 and 8 years and suspected ASD among children aged 4 years. Surveillance included children who lived in the surveillance area at any time during 2022. Children were classified as having ASD if they ever received 1) an ASD diagnostic statement in a comprehensive developmental evaluation, 2) autism special education eligibility, or 3) an ASD International Classification of Diseases, Ninth Revision (ICD-9) code in the 299 range or International Classification of Diseases, Tenth Revision (ICD-10) code of F84.0, F84.3, F84.5, F84.8, or F84.9. Children aged 4 years were classified as having suspected ASD if they did not meet the case definition for ASD but had an evaluator's suspicion of ASD documented in a comprehensive developmental evaluation.

**RESULTS:** Among children aged 8 years in 2022, ASD prevalence was 32.2 per 1,000 children (one in 31) across the 16 sites, ranging from 9.7 in Texas (Laredo) to 53.1 in California. The overall observed prevalence estimate was similar to estimates calculated using Bayesian hierarchical and random effects models. ASD was 3.4 times as prevalent among boys (49.2) than girls (14.3). Overall, ASD prevalence was lower among non-Hispanic White (White) children (27.7) than among Asian or Pacific Islander (A/PI) (38.2), American Indian or Alaska Native (AI/AN) (37.5), non-Hispanic Black or African American (Black) (36.6), Hispanic or Latino (Hispanic) (33.0), and multiracial children (31.9). No association was observed between ASD prevalence and neighborhood median household income (MHI) at 11 sites; higher ASD prevalence was associated with lower neighborhood MHI at five sites.Record abstraction was completed for 15 of the 16 sites for 8,613 children aged 8 years who met the ASD case definition. Of these 8,613 children, 68.4% had a documented diagnostic statement of ASD, 67.3% had a documented autism special education eligibility, and 68.9% had a documented ASD ICD-9 or ICD-10 code. All three elements of the ASD case definition were present for 34.6% of children aged 8 years with ASD.Among 5,292 (61.4% of 8,613) children aged 8 years with ASD with information on cognitive ability, 39.6% were classified as having an intellectual disability. Intellectual disability was present among 52.8% of Black, 50.0% of AI/AN, 43.9% of A/PI, 38.8% of Hispanic, 32.7% of White, and 31.2% of multiracial children with ASD. The median age of earliest known ASD diagnosis was 47 months and ranged from 36 months in California to 69.5 months in Texas (Laredo).Cumulative incidence of ASD diagnosis or eligibility by age 48 months was higher among children born in 2018 (aged 4 years in 2022) than children born in 2014 (aged 8 years in 2022) at 13 of the 15 sites that were able to abstract records. Overall cumulative incidence of ASD diagnosis or eligibility by age 48 months was 1.7 times as high among those born in 2018 compared with those born in 2014 and ranged from 1.4 times as high in Arizona and Georgia to 3.1 times as high in Puerto Rico. Among children aged 4 years, for every 10 children meeting the case definition of ASD, one child met the definition of suspected ASD.Children with ASD who were born in 2018 had more evaluations and identification during ages 0-4 years than children with ASD who were born in 2014 during the 0-4 years age window, with an interruption in the pattern in early 2020 coinciding with onset of the COVID-19 pandemic.Overall, 66.5% of children aged 8 years with ASD had a documented autism test. Use of autism tests varied widely across sites: 24.7% (New Jersey) to 93.5% (Puerto Rico) of children aged 8 years with ASD had a documented autism test in their records. The most common tests documented for children aged 8 years were the Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule, Autism Spectrum Rating Scales, Childhood Autism Rating Scale, Gilliam Autism Rating Scale, and Social Responsiveness Scale.

**INTERPRETATION:** Prevalence of ASD among children aged 8 years was higher in 2022 than previous years. ASD prevalence was higher among A/PI, Black, and Hispanic children aged 8 years than White children aged 8 years, continuing a pattern first observed in 2020. A/PI, Black, and Hispanic children aged 8 years with ASD were also more likely than White or multiracial children with ASD to have a co-occurring intellectual disability. Identification by age 48 months was higher among children born in 2018 compared with children born in 2014, suggesting increased early identification consistent with historical patterns.

**PUBLIC HEALTH ACTION:** Increased identification of autism, particularly among very young children and previously underidentified groups, underscores the increased demand and ongoing need for enhanced planning to provide equitable diagnostic, treatment, and support services for all children with ASD. The substantial variability in ASD identification across sites suggests opportunities to identify and implement successful strategies and practices in communities to ensure all children with ASD reach their potential.

## Key findings

- **Overall prevalence (8-year-olds, 2022): 32.2 per 1,000 = 1 in 31**, across 16 ADDM sites; site range 9.7 (Texas-Laredo) to 53.1 (California), a ~5.5-fold spread. Bayesian hierarchical estimate 32.2 (95% credible interval 25.4–39.3); random-effects REML 30.9 (95% CI 25.8–36.0).
- **Rising trend:** prevalence climbed from 1 in 150 (2000) → 1 in 36 (2020) → 1 in 31 (2022). Among the 11 sites surveilled in both 2020 and 2022, prevalence was 22.2% higher overall (absolute +6.1 per 1,000); the +6.1 absolute increase held even when restricted to the 5 sites with unchanged boundaries/data sources (22.8% higher).
- **Sex difference:** male-to-female prevalence ratio 3.4 (boys 49.2 vs girls 14.3 per 1,000), with significant site heterogeneity (Mantel-Haenszel). The ratio has *narrowed* over time (4.2 in 2018 → 3.8 in 2020 → 3.4 in 2022), but the absolute boy–girl gap *widened* (27.7 → 31.7 → 34.9 per 1,000). Co-occurring intellectual disability was nearly equal by sex (girls 40.4% vs boys 39.5%).
- **Race/ethnicity reversal:** White children had the *lowest* prevalence (27.7) vs A/PI 38.2, AI/AN 37.5, Black 36.6, Hispanic 33.0, multiracial 31.9 — continuing the inversion of the pre-2016 pattern (highest in White / high-SES). Utah was the only site where another group (multiracial) was below White.
- **Co-occurring intellectual disability (ID, IQ ≤70):** among 5,292 (61.4% of 8,613) 8-year-olds with cognitive data, **39.6% had ID**, 24.2% borderline (IQ 71–85), 36.1% average/higher (>85). ID burden differed by race/ethnicity: Black 52.8%, AI/AN 50.0%, A/PI 43.9%, Hispanic 38.8%, White 32.7%, multiracial 31.2%.
- **Earlier identification in younger cohort:** cumulative incidence of ASD diagnosis/eligibility by **age 48 months was 1.7× higher** in the 2018 birth cohort (22.6 per 1,000) vs the 2014 cohort (13.1 per 1,000); pattern held at 13 of 16 sites (range 1.4× Arizona/Georgia to 3.1× Puerto Rico).
- **Age at first diagnosis:** median earliest known ASD diagnosis **47 months** (range 36 mo California to 69.5 mo Texas-Laredo); children with ID were diagnosed earlier (43 mo) than those without (49 mo). Only 50.3% of 8-year-olds with ASD were evaluated by age 36 months.
- **Case-definition components (n=8,613, 15 sites):** 68.4% had an ASD diagnostic statement, 67.3% autism special-education eligibility, 68.9% an ICD code; only **34.6% (34.7%) had all three**; 69.9% had ≥2 of 3; 9.4% met the definition by ICD code alone. Prevalence based *only* on diagnostic statements was 22.5 per 1,000 (vs 32.2 full definition).
- **COVID-19:** the 2018 cohort's early-identification advantage was briefly disrupted in March–April 2020 (first 2 months post-declaration) and resumed by June 2020; no *sustained* pandemic effect detected. Telehealth assessment appeared in 8.7% of the younger cohort's records.
- **Suspected ASD (4-year-olds):** prevalence 3.1 per 1,000 — ~1 suspected case for every 10 identified ASD cases, indicating few clinicians take a "wait and see" stance after comprehensive evaluation.

## Methods

- **System:** CDC Autism and Developmental Disabilities Monitoring (ADDM) Network — active, records-based surveillance; biennial 8-year-old ASD estimates since 2000, 4-year-old tracking since 2010. 2022 cycle = 16 sites (11 from Jan 2023, +5 in April via 2023 Consolidated Appropriations Act expansion funds).
- **Population/denominators:** children born 2014 (aged 8) or 2018 (aged 4) resident in surveillance area at any point in 2022; denominators from U.S. Census Bureau Vintage 2022 postcensal estimates (4 partial-county sites adjusted with ACS).
- **Case ascertainment:** linkage of health-source records (developmental evaluations, ICD-9 [299.x] / ICD-10 [F84.0/.3/.5/.8/.9] codes, Medicaid/disability claims) and education-source records (autism special-education eligibility). Record abstraction at 15 sites (Indiana excluded from abstraction analyses — only had eligibility-category + ICD data, no diagnostic statements; 8,613 abstracted 8-year-old cases).
- **Analysis:** prevalence per 1,000; Bayesian hierarchical model + random-effects REML for overall estimate with uncertainty; Wilson-score 95% CIs; Mantel-Haenszel homogeneity test for sex ratio; Cochran-Armitage trend tests across MHI / CDC social vulnerability index (SVI) tertiles; cumulative incidence and risk ratios by month of age; COVID effect assessed by month-matched difference between the two birth cohorts over the age 0–4 window. ID defined as IQ ≤70 or examiner statement. Estimates with relative SE ≥30% suppressed. R v4.4.1.

## Relevance to the brain-organoid ASD review

- **Frames the clinical/public-health stakes (manuscript opening "proactive" motivation):** 1-in-31 prevalence and a still-rising trend quantify the scale that a "proactive autism genetics" program addresses — the headline epidemiological anchor for why pre-emptive variant interpretation and organoid modeling matter.
- **Sex differences theme (5):** the persistent 3.4:1 male:female ratio, with the absolute gap *widening* even as the ratio narrows, motivates the review's sex-difference section and the "female protective effect" discussion that organoid/variant-effect platforms can probe mechanistically. Pairs with Iossifov 2014 (female-enriched de novo burden) and Satterstrom X-chromosome work in this collection.
- **Early developmental window (theme 2):** median diagnosis at 47 months and rising identification by 48 months underscore that the *causally relevant* neurodevelopmental events precede diagnosis by years — supporting the manuscript's argument for modeling early/prenatal developmental vulnerability stages in organoids rather than studying established phenotypes.
- **Heterogeneity / ID stratification:** the 39.6% co-occurring intellectual disability rate and its variation links phenotypic severity to the high- vs moderate-penetrance gene distinction (Zhou 2022, Iossifov 2014) the review uses to argue for genotype-stratified organoid models.
- **Caveat for genetic interpretation:** this is a *surveillance/epidemiology* source — it provides population prevalence, sex ratios, and ID co-occurrence but **contains no molecular genetic data** (no variants, genes, or heritability). Cite it for burden/demographics, not for genetic architecture claims.

## Open questions / limitations

- Surveillance prevalence reflects **identification/ascertainment**, not necessarily true incidence; ~5.5-fold site variation is attributed to differences in service access, screening initiatives (e.g., California Get SET Early; Puerto Rico early-ID programs) and diagnostic practice, not differential risk. The authors explicitly note research has not shown community of residence raises ASD risk.
- Race/ethnicity and SES prevalence inversions are interpreted as improving equity of access, but residual disparities persist (e.g., 2020 data still showed higher MHI → higher prevalence within A/PI, Black, Hispanic groups); low Texas-site prevalence may reflect access barriers in primarily Hispanic, lower-MHI communities.
- Cognitive-ability data available for only 61.4% of cases; ID estimates depend on which children get IQ tested (range 21.3%–90.2% across sites), biasing severity comparisons.
- Indiana lacked diagnostic-statement data (excluded from abstraction analyses); three sites had <100% education-source coverage (Georgia 97.3%, Missouri 68.0%, Texas-Laredo 99.7%).
- Two age cohorts (born 2014 vs 2018) only; long-term and adult prevalence not captured. No etiologic, genetic, or biomarker data collected.
