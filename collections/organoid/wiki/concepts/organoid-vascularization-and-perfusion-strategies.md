# Organoid vascularization and perfusion strategies

## Current position in this corpus

This corpus now contains five distinct vascularization strategies: stand-alone blood-vessel organoids, transcription-factor-driven vascular co-development inside brain organoids, flow-assisted vascular maturation in kidney organoids, mesodermal-progenitor-based vascular incorporation across organoid types, and orthogonal transcription-factor activation for dedicated human vascular organoids.

## Working synthesis

- This page is the main entry point when the question is **which vascularization route fits the organ and endpoint**.
- The corpus now separates several moves that used to collapse into one word: stand-alone vessel organoids, TF-driven vascular induction inside a host tissue, flow or perfusion rescue, mesodermal co-development, and later host integration.
- Wimmer anchors vessel-only baselines, Cakir anchors integrated brain support, Homan anchors kidney perfusion or maturation rescue, Worsdorfer anchors reusable multicompartment mesodermal mixing, and L 2026 extends the vessel-only branch into more explicit transcriptional programming.
- A useful rule for this page is therefore: **come here first when vascularization is the missing layer, but the harder question is whether you need a vessel module, an organ-support module, a perfusion module, or a host-integration endpoint**.

## Main tensions

- engineering control versus physiological self-organization
- in vitro perfusion and screening-readiness versus reliance on transplantation for fuller maturation
- organ-specific optimization versus reusable general vascular modules

## Open questions

- Which vascularization strategy gives the best tradeoff among adoption cost, vessel maturity, and compatibility with sensitive organoid identities?
- When does transplantation still outperform purely in vitro vascularization or perfusion?
- Can vascularization be standardized enough for screening workflows across multiple organ systems?
