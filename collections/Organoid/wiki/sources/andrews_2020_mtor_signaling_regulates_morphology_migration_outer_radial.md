# mTOR signaling regulates the morphology and migration of outer radial glia in developing human cortex.

## Source

- Authors: Andrews MG, Subramanian L (co-first authors), Kriegstein AR (UCSF Dept. of Neurology / Eli & Edythe Broad Center).
- Journal: eLife 2020;9:e58737 (published 2 September 2020). CC-BY.
- DOI: [10.7554/eLife.58737](https://doi.org/10.7554/eLife.58737)
- PMID: [32876565](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32876565/)
- Added via: manuscript_brain_organoid_v6 reference ingest

## Abstract

Outer radial glial (oRG) cells are a population of neural stem cells prevalent in the developing human cortex that contribute to its cellular diversity and evolutionary expansion. The mammalian Target of Rapamycin (mTOR) signaling pathway is active in human oRG cells. Mutations in mTOR pathway genes are linked to a variety of neurodevelopmental disorders and malformations of cortical development. We find that dysregulation of mTOR signaling specifically affects oRG cells, but not other progenitor types, by changing the actin cytoskeleton through the activity of the Rho-GTPase, CDC42. These effects change oRG cellular morphology, migration, and mitotic behavior, but do not affect proliferation or cell fate. Thus, mTOR signaling can regulate the architecture of the developing human cortex by maintaining the cytoskeletal organization of oRG cells and the radial glia scaffold. Our study provides insight into how mTOR dysregulation may contribute to neurodevelopmental disease.

## Key findings

- **mTORC1 activity is cell-type-selective: high in oRG cells, near-absent in other progenitors.** Phospho-S6 (pS6, a mTORC1 readout) co-localizes with HOPX+/SOX2+ oRG cells in the outer subventricular zone (oSVZ) of GW15–19 human cortex, but is absent from ventricular radial glia (vRG, SOX2+ VZ), truncated radial glia (CRYAB+ tRG), intermediate progenitors (TBR2+ IPCs), and neurons. Among neural progenitors, only oSVZ oRG cells (and laminin+ blood vessels / some microglia) show mTOR activity (n = 28 sections, 3 individuals; SOX2+ VZ vs oSVZ **P < 0.0031). Same pattern holds in 8-week cortical organoids.
- **Both inhibiting and hyperactivating mTOR truncate the oRG basal process** (a bimodal/"Goldilocks" effect). In organotypic slices (GW15–19) treated with rapamycin (inhibit) or BDNF/3BDO (hyperactivate), the radial glial scaffold was disrupted and HOPX+ oRG basal-process length significantly reduced (all ****P < 0.0001); rapamycin co-treatment reversed BDNF effects, confirming specificity. The primary process also lost its perpendicular (61–90°) orientation toward the pia, becoming oblique (31–60°) or parallel (0–30°).
- **Effect is specific to oRG cells.** No process-length change in vRG, tRG, or IPCs after mTOR manipulation. Replicated in week-10 cortical organoids (HOPX+ process shortened; n = 141–171 cells/group) and with cell-autonomous shRNA (RPTOR shRNA to inhibit, TSC2 shRNA to hyperactivate): both shortened oRG processes in slices and organoids.
- **No change in oRG number, fate, proliferation, or death.** mTOR manipulation did not alter HOPX+ oRG counts, fate of vRG/IPCs/CTIP2/SATB2/CUX1 neurons (slices), BrdU incorporation, pH-H3, or cleaved-caspase-3. (Organoids showed inconsistent, line-dependent number changes deemed unreliable.) → mTOR maintains oRG **morphology, not specification/proliferation**.
- **mTOR dysregulation impairs oRG migration and mitotic somal translocation (MST).** RPTOR or TSC2 shRNA oRG cells migrated significantly shorter distances from the VZ edge (both ****P < 0.0001; n > 27 cells/group). By live imaging of dissociated oRG, rapamycin left MST frequency intact but shortened the MST "jump" distance (**P = 0.0082) and the pre-division migration distance (**P = 0.0058), plus induced ectopic extra processes.
- **Mechanism: mTOR → actin cytoskeleton via CDC42 (and RAC1).** mTOR inhibition/hyperactivation reduced F-actin (phalloidin; rapamycin ***P < 0.0002, BDNF ****P < 0.0001) and lowered CDC42 protein plus its effectors CFL1 (cofilin) and ACTR2 (Arp2). The Rho-GTPase activator **CN04 (500 ng/mL)** restored CDC42/CFL1/ACTR2 levels and activated CDC42 and RAC1 (CDC42 ~2× more than RAC1) but **not RHOA**.
- **CDC42/RAC1 activation rescues the oRG defect downstream of mTOR.** Co-treating rapamycin-inhibited slices with CN04 produced a **complete rescue of both basal-process truncation and the migration defect** (rapamycin+CN04 process length P = 0.5097 vs control) — even though pS6 stayed low (no restoration of mTOR activity). This places Rho-GTPase/actin regulation downstream of mTOR.
- **Division of labor in the actomyosin system:** Rho-ROCK/non-muscle-myosin-II governs the MST event itself, whereas actin-modulating GTPases (CDC42/RAC1) control migration before/around mitosis; mTOR acts on the latter without affecting RHOA/MST occurrence.

## Methods

- **Three complementary human systems:** (1) organotypic slice cultures of primary GW15–19 cortex (air-liquid interface, 300 µm sections, ~6–7 days); (2) dissociated primary cortical progenitors with timelapse imaging (Leica SP5, 20-min intervals, up to 3 days) to score MST/migration; (3) PSC-derived **cortical organoids** (Kadoshima/Pollen forebrain protocol; lines H1/WA01, 13234, H28126) treated from week 5 and collected at weeks 8 and 10.
- **mTOR manipulation reagents:** rapamycin **250 nM** (inhibit), BDNF **100 ng/mL** and 3BDO **1 mM** (hyperactivate); cell-autonomous shRNAs (RPTOR_1, pLKO.1-TSC2; scramble control) electroporated at 1 µg/µl; Rho-GTPase activator **CN04 500 ng/mL**.
- **Readouts:** pS6/HOPX/SOX2/TBR2/CRYAB immunostaining; HOPX+ basal-fiber length traced in Imaris (z-stacks, normalized to slice height); migration distance from VZ edge; live-imaging MST (defined as ≥1 soma-diameter translocation at ≥20 µm/hr velocity); G-LISA ELISA assays for CDC42/RAC1/RHOA activity; phalloidin-rhodamine for F-actin.
- **Statistics:** D'Agostino-Pearson normality → one-way ANOVA (normal) or Kruskal-Wallis (non-normal); two-condition comparisons by unpaired two-tailed t-test; significance p < 0.05.
- Authors note organoids do not replicate all cortical organization/metabolism but reproduce the mTOR expression pattern and functional effect, validating them as a tractable model for follow-up.

## Relevance to the brain-organoid ASD review

- **Direct support for the review's "oRG biology (mTOR)" theme.** Establishes that human oRG cells — the human-enriched progenitor driving cortical expansion and the radial scaffold — are **uniquely vulnerable to mTOR dysregulation**, which the review can frame as a selective-vulnerability node in mTORopathies/ASD.
- **Disease link:** mTOR pathway genes (e.g. TSC1/2, DEPDC5, PTEN) are mutated in ASD, Fragile X, focal cortical dysplasia, and tuberous sclerosis; this paper supplies the human cell-biological mechanism (cytoskeletal CDC42/actin control of oRG morphology and migration) connecting those mutations to malformations of cortical development.
- **Therapeutic/rescue proof-of-concept for "perturbation in 3D":** the CN04 (CDC42/RAC1) rescue shows the oRG phenotype is reversible downstream of mTOR — a perturbation-and-rescue logic the review can cite alongside genetic/pharmacological rescue in neurons (cf. SHANK3/IGF-1 in [Shcheglovitov 2013](shcheglovitov_2013_shank3_igf1_restore_synaptic_deficits_neurons_22q13.md)).
- **Why organoids matter:** demonstrates a phenotype concordant between primary human tissue and PSC-derived organoids, justifying organoids as the scalable, genetically tractable platform for the review's proposed programmable-perturbation pipeline targeting progenitor (oRG) biology.
- Complements TSC/mTOR organoid modeling already in the corpus ([Blair 2018](blair_2018_genetically_engineered_human_cortical_spheroid_models_tuberous.md)) and PTEN-ASD work ([Fu 2023](fu_2023_autism_specific_pten_p_ile135leu_variant_autism.md), [Kang 2024](kang_2024_germline_pten_genotype_dependent_phenotypic_divergence_during.md)).

## Open questions / limitations

- **Which mTOR complex transduces the effect is unresolved.** Cytoskeletal phenotypes resemble mTORC2 output, yet RPTOR shRNA (mTORC1-specific) reproduces them; authors propose mTORC1 translational/local-mRNA control (e.g. of β-actin) in basal processes, possibly with mTORC2, but cannot disentangle them (prolonged rapamycin can also perturb mTORC2 assembly).
- **The bimodal (too-much / too-little) mechanism is unexplained** at the molecular level — likely stoichiometric disruption of multi-protein mTOR complexes; not directly demonstrated here.
- **Limited, scarce primary tissue:** small n for live-imaging/electroporation (some experiments only 2 individuals); organoid cell-number data were inconsistent across PSC lines and judged unreliable.
- Effects shown over short- and medium-term culture; long-term consequences for cortical lamination/folding and links to specific patient mutations remain to be tested in organoids.
- Does not address whether oRG-specific mTOR vulnerability produces downstream ASD-relevant circuit phenotypes (neuronal migration along the disrupted scaffold is implied but not assayed functionally).
