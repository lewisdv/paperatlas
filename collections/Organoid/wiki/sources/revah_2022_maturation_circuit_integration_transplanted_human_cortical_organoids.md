# Maturation and circuit integration of transplanted human cortical organoids.

## Abstract

Self-organizing neural organoids represent a promising in vitro platform with which to model human development and disease1-5. However, organoids lack the connectivity that exists in vivo, which limits maturation and makes integration with other circuits that control behaviour impossible. Here we show that human stem cell-derived cortical organoids transplanted into the somatosensory cortex of newborn athymic rats develop mature cell types that integrate into sensory and motivation-related circuits. MRI reveals post-transplantation organoid growth across multiple stem cell lines and animals, whereas single-nucleus profiling shows progression of corticogenesis and the emergence of activity-dependent transcriptional programs. Indeed, transplanted cortical neurons display more complex morphological, synaptic and intrinsic membrane properties than their in vitro counterparts, which enables the discovery of defects in neurons derived from individuals with Timothy syndrome. Anatomical and functional tracings show that transplanted organoids receive thalamocortical and corticocortical inputs, and in vivo recordings of neural activity demonstrate that these inputs can produce sensory responses in human cells. Finally, cortical organoids extend axons throughout the rat brain and their optogenetic activation can drive reward-seeking behaviour. Thus, transplanted human cortical neurons mature and engage host circuits that control behaviour. We anticipate that this approach will be useful for detecting circuit-level phenotypes in patient-derived cells that cannot otherwise be uncovered.

## Key findings

Intact hiPSC-derived human cortical organoids (hCO) transplanted into the primary somatosensory cortex (S1) of newborn (postnatal day 3-7) athymic rats undergo substantial in-vivo maturation and integrate both anatomically and functionally into host sensory and motivation circuits. Transplanted hCO (t-hCO) reach states unattainable in vitro and expose an activity-dependent disease phenotype in Timothy syndrome (TS) neurons.

- **Engraftment reliability (across-line validation).** t-hCO were identified in **81% of engrafted animals** at ~2 months post-transplantation (n = 72 animals; hCO from **10 hiPS cell lines**); of these, **87% were located in the cerebral cortex**. Per-line success quantified across n = 108 grafts. Transplanted-animal survival was **74% at 12 months**, with no detectable locomotor/memory deficits, gliosis, or EEG abnormalities.
- **Graft growth (live MRI).** Serial T2-weighted MRI in the same rats showed t-hCO increased **~9-fold in volume over 3 months**; MRI volumes matched fixed-slice volumes (P > 0.05).
- **Host-supplied microenvironment.** Grafts were vascularized by rat endothelia and infiltrated by rat IBA1+ microglia. snRNA-seq at ~8 months (**21,500 high-quality human nuclei**, n = 3 t-hCO samples, 2 lines) recovered deep- and superficial-layer glutamatergic neurons, cycling progenitors, oligodendrocytes (absent in vitro), and astrocyte-lineage cells. Despite layer subtypes, **t-hCO showed no obvious anatomical lamination**.
- **Advanced morphology/electrophysiology vs in vitro hCO** (7-8 months differentiation): soma **1.5x larger** in diameter, **~2x** more dendrites, **~6x** total dendrite length, higher spine density; membrane capacitance **~8x higher**; resting membrane potential ~20 mV more hyperpolarized; higher maximal firing rates; significantly higher spontaneous EPSC rate.
- **Transcriptomic maturation.** t-hCO glutamatergic neurons upregulated synaptic-signalling/dendrite/voltage-gated-channel gene sets and activity-regulated genes (late-response genes BDNF, SCG2, and primate-specific OSTN). Global maturation of both hCO and t-hCO at 7-8 months was **most equivalent to the late fetal period**, with t-hCO advanced over age-matched hCO; t-hCO glutamatergic clusters overlapped adult L2/3, L5, L6 subclasses but had limited overlap with second-trimester fetal neurons.
- **Resembles postnatal human L2/3 neurons.** t-hCO pyramidal-neuron electrophysiology matched acute-slice postnatal human L2/3 pyramidal neurons; morphology was much closer to human L2/3 than in-vitro hCO (human cells still longer, more branched, higher spine density).
- **Disease phenotype unmasked by maturation (Timothy syndrome).** hCO from 3 TS patients (CaV1.2 p.G406R, CACNA1C) vs 3 controls. TS t-hCO neurons showed **~2x more primary dendrites**, reduced mean/total dendrite length, increased spine density and higher spontaneous EPSC frequency, and abnormal Sholl branching. Critically, the abnormal branching appeared **only in vivo (t-hCO), not in stage-matched in-vitro TS hCO**, consistent with activity-dependent dendritic retraction.
- **Afferent (host -> graft) integration.** Rabies-dG-GFP/AAV-G tracing showed inputs from ipsilateral S1 and ventrobasal (VB) thalamus; netrin G1+ thalamic terminals present in t-hCO. Electrical or optogenetic stimulation of rat S1/internal-capsule/thalamic fibres evoked short-latency, NBQX-blockable EPSCs in human cells.
- **Sensory responsiveness in vivo.** GCaMP6s t-hCO (imaged ~150 days post-transplant) showed synchronous rhythmic activity. Extracellular recordings: synchronous bursts ~460 ms long separated by ~2 s silent periods, ~3 spikes/burst, each burst recruiting ~73% of units; correlations exceeded those in non-transplanted controls. Optotagged (hChR2, <10 ms light response) human units: **~54% significantly increased firing after contralateral whisker deflection**, peaking ~650 ms post-stimulus (vs randomized timestamps).
- **Efferent (graft -> host) integration and behaviour.** hChR2-EYFP t-hCO extended axons to ipsilateral auditory/motor/somatosensory cortex and striatum, hippocampus, thalamus (~110 days). Optogenetic activation of t-hCO axons evoked short-latency EPSCs in rat cortical neurons (mean latency 7.13 ms; monosynaptic, TTX-blocked/4-AP-recovered, NBQX-blocked). In an operant task rewarding licking during blue (vs red) light, **hChR2 animals learned the association (success 89%, n = 9) vs 0% in EYFP controls (n = 9)**; optogenetic t-hCO activation induced FOS in mPFC, mediodorsal thalamus, periaqueductal grey.

## Methods

- **Organoid generation.** hCO from hiPS cells via dual-SMAD inhibition (dorsomorphin + SB-431542) + ROCK inhibitor Y-27632; EGF/FGF2 (each 20 ng/ml) to day 24; BDNF + NT3 (each 20 ng/ml) days 25-42; unsupplemented neurobasal-A thereafter (Pașca-lab hCO/hCS protocol). Both feeder and feeder-free (AggreWell 800, 3x10^6 cells/well) starts used.
- **Transplantation.** Intact 3D hCO at **days 30-60 of differentiation** stereotaxically delivered into S1 of **postnatal day 3-7 athymic (FOXN1-/-) rat pups** through a ~2-3 mm craniotomy and a ~0.3 mm dural puncture; organoid ejected via Hamilton syringe at 1-2 µl/min, needle retracted 0.2-0.5 mm/min; some animals engrafted bilaterally. Early-postnatal timing chosen so thalamocortical/corticocortical projections have not yet completed S1 innervation (maximize integration, minimize disruption of endogenous circuitry).
- **Readouts.** Live T2-weighted MRI + 3D volume reconstruction; snRNA-seq (Seurat integration; label transfer from adult human motor-cortex reference); biocytin-fill morphology + Sholl; whole-cell patch-clamp in acute (thalamocortical) slices with NBQX/TTX/4-AP; rabies monosynaptic tracing; netrin G1/STEM121/HNA immunostaining; fibre photometry and two-photon GCaMP6s imaging; extracellular recording with ChR2 optotagging; whisker-deflection sensory stimulation; operant optogenetic conditioning; FOS immunohistochemistry. Comparators: stage-matched in-vitro hCO; acute-slice postnatal human cortex; fetal/adult cortical transcriptomic references.
- **Caveat noted by authors:** extracellular units are "putative human neurons" by MRI-guided coordinates; electrophysiology alone cannot assign species of origin (addressed via ChR2 optotagging).

## Relevance to the brain-organoid ASD review

- **Primary citation for the "transplantation & host-circuit integration / in-vivo maturation validation" theme.** This is the strongest demonstration that an intact human cortical organoid, once placed in a developing host cortex, acquires synaptic, intrinsic, transcriptional, and morphological maturity that in-vitro culture does not reach, and becomes wired into bona fide host sensory (thalamocortical) input and motivation-related output circuits.
- **Behavioural-level phenotyping for genetically defined disease.** The operant reward-seeking result establishes that human-organoid activity can be linked to a host behavioural output - the capability a "programmable / proactive ASD genetics" review needs to argue that ASD-risk variants could eventually be read out at the circuit/behaviour level, not just in dish.
- **Direct ASD proof-of-concept via Timothy syndrome.** TS is an ASD-associated channelopathy (CACNA1C/CaV1.2). The key methodological lesson for the review: an **activity-dependent dendritic phenotype was revealed only after in-vivo maturation** and was invisible in stage-matched in-vitro organoids - a concrete cautionary argument that some ASD-relevant circuit phenotypes require host-context maturation to surface.
- **Cross-line reliability (81% engraftment over 10 lines; live MRI tracking) supports** using transplantation as a scalable validation layer behind dish-based ASD screens.

## Open questions / limitations

- Authors state temporospatial and cross-species limits preclude high-fidelity human circuit formation even with early transplantation.
- **No lamination** in t-hCO; unclear how missing cortical layering affects circuit connectivity.
- Spontaneous synchronous activity may be developmental, or may reflect the **lack of inhibitory (GABAergic) cell types** in t-hCO - relevant because excitatory/inhibitory imbalance is central to ASD.
- Grafts depend on host vasculature, host microglia, and a xenogeneic (rat) circuit; human microglia, human endothelial cells, and defined interneuron proportions are not yet incorporated (proposed future work via assembloid approaches).
- Single-graft "unit" of human cortex without normal areal context; species-of-origin for some electrophysiological units inferred rather than proven (mitigated by optotagging).
