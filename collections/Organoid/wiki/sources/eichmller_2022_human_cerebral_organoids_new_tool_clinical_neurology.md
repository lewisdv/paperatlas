## Abstract

The current understanding of neurological diseases is derived mostly from direct analysis of patients and from animal models of disease. However, most patient studies do not capture the earliest stages of disease development and offer limited opportunities for experimental intervention, so rarely yield complete mechanistic insights. The use of animal models relies on evolutionary conservation of pathways involved in disease and is limited by an inability to recreate human-specific processes. In vitro models that are derived from human pluripotent stem cells cultured in 3D have emerged as a new model system that could bridge the gap between patient studies and animal models. In this Review, we summarize how such organoid models can complement classical approaches to accelerate neurological research. We describe our current understanding of neurodevelopment and how this process differs between humans and other animals, making human-derived models of disease essential. We discuss different methodologies for producing organoids and how organoids can be and have been used to model neurological disorders, including microcephaly, Zika virus infection, Alzheimer disease and other neurodegenerative disorders, and neurodevelopmental diseases, such as Timothy syndrome, Angelman syndrome and tuberous sclerosis. We also discuss the current limitations of organoid models and outline how organoids can be used to revolutionize research into the human brain and neurological diseases.

## Key findings

(Review by Eichmüller & Knoblich, IMBA/Medical University of Vienna; *Nat Rev Neurol* 18:661-680, Nov 2022.)

- **Core thesis — organoids bridge patient studies and animal models.** Patient studies (sequencing, imaging, post-mortem) mostly give snapshots and miss disease *initiation*; animal models allow controlled, mechanistic study but depend on conserved disease mechanisms that are often broken by human-specific neurodevelopment. Human 3D organoids fill the gap by enabling experimental access to *early* human-tissue pathophysiology (Fig 1).
- **Human-specific neurodevelopment is the central justification for human models.** The review catalogs processes absent or weak in rodents: an expanded **outer radial glia (oRGC)** population in the outer SVZ that produces most human cortical neurons (with human-specific mTOR/Notch/PDGF/Robo regulation; differs even between human and chimp/macaque); a dramatically expanded **subplate** (>4x thicker than cortical plate at mid-gestation) orchestrating connectivity; expansion of upper layers II/III; protracted, postnatal-extending **interneuron** generation/migration (the "Arc"), with GABAergic neurons up to ~30% of neurons in association cortices (vs <15% in mouse) and CGE-enriched upper-layer interneurons; and human-specific cerebellar rhombic-lip biology. Human-specific genes/duplications (ARHGAP11B, NOTCH2NL, TBC1D3, PPP1R17, HARE5) drive progenitor expansion.
- **Methodology taxonomy.** hCOs derive from ES/iPSCs -> embryoid bodies -> neural induction -> ECM embedding/proliferation; first neurons by ~day 20, postnatal features after >1 year. **Unguided** protocols (no morphogens) give mixed regional identities; **guided** protocols add patterning factors for homogeneous region-specific organoids; **assembloids** fuse differentially patterned organoids to study interactions (e.g. ventral->dorsal interneuron migration, axonal projections); and — relevant to the survival/maturation theme — **sliced, air-liquid-interface cerebral organoids (ALI-COs)** are highlighted as a way to overcome restricted nutrient supply (Fig 3d).
- **Two strategies for disease modeling.** (1) Introduce known risk variants (incl. CRISPR mosaic/high-throughput screens) to test causal genotype->phenotype links; (2) generate organoids from patient iPSCs to preserve the individual's genetic background. A 2020 mosaic-CRISPR screen of **172 microcephaly (MCPH) genes** in barcoded organoids found 32 genes reducing proliferation, including a novel ER-stress/ECM-protein pathway.
- **Disease examples where organoids beat or complement animal models** (Table 1): microcephaly (CDK5RAP2, ASPM — phenotypes weak/variable in mice but strong in human organoids/ferrets); **Zika virus** microcephaly (3D architecture required; NS2A disrupts radial-glia apical junctions; recapitulates type-I-IFN suppression seen in humans but not mice); Alzheimer disease (familial APP/PSEN1/PSEN2 and Down syndrome organoids develop Aβ/tau pathology by ~2 months vs ~6 months for sporadic/APOE*ε4; microglia and vasculature increasingly incorporated); frontotemporal dementia (MAPT) via autophagy-lysosomal/ELAVL4 splitting; and neurodevelopmental syndromes.
- **Neurodevelopmental/ASD-relevant exemplars.** **Timothy syndrome** (CACNA1C/CaV1.2): ventral-dorsal assembloids reveal interneuron saltatory-migration defects — decreased saltation length (actin/calcium) and increased frequency (GABA-receptor signaling). **Angelman syndrome** (UBE3A): organoids identify BK potassium channels as the UBE3A substrate; paxilline reverses increased network synchronicity (validated back in mice). **Tuberous sclerosis** (TSC1/TSC2): Knoblich-lab unguided organoids identify caudal late interneuron progenitors (**CLIP cells**) as the heterozygous disease-initiating population generating tubers and tumors.
- **ASD modeling specifically.** ASD is highly heritable but polygenic (>100 genes, converging on transcription/synaptogenesis); patient-iPSC organoids capture combined low-risk/unknown variants on the patient's own background. Cited work (e.g. Mariani-type idiopathic-macrocephalic ASD) shows excitatory/inhibitory imbalance; another shows divergent mechanisms in ASD-with- vs without-macrocephaly that nonetheless converge within each cohort, with shared dysregulation of **oRGCs**. Mid-fetal-period processes and upper-layer projection neurons are implicated; dense temporal scRNA + chromatin sampling in organoids + high-throughput screens are framed as the path to dissecting ASD risk-gene timing and cell-type specificity.
- **Translational/clinical framework (Fig 5).** A 9-step loop couples patient research <-> organoids: clinician collaboration -> patient research -> risk-gene screening in organoids -> causative-gene identification -> (if conserved) mouse models -> patient-derived organoids -> drug testing -> transplantation for in-vivo evaluation -> disease-mechanism insight feeding back to patient care. Organoids' niche is early disease initiation/early pathophysiology in human tissue.

## Relevance to the brain-organoid ASD review

- **Authoritative secondary anchor for the review's clinical-translation framing of cerebral organoids.** As a *Nature Reviews Neurology* piece from the Knoblich lab (co-originator of cerebral organoids), it supplies the high-level "organoids bridge patient and animal studies / accelerate clinical neurology" narrative and the patient-care feedback loop (Fig 5) that the ASD review can cite for its translational thesis.
- **Justifies the human-model imperative for ASD via human-specific biology.** Its detailed account of oRGCs, subplate expansion, protracted interneuron development and human-specific genes underpins the argument that ASD mechanisms (which converge on these human-amplified processes) require human organoids rather than mouse models.
- **Frames the advanced platforms the ASD review foregrounds.** It explicitly names sliced **ALI-COs** ([Giandomenico 2019](giandomenico_2019_cerebral_organoids_at_air_liquid_interface_generate.md)) as a nutrient-supply solution and discusses vascularization (co-culture/induction/transplantation) and region-specific protocols as reproducibility improvements — the same gap-filling space as the chip/ECM work of [Cho 2021](cho_2021_microfluidic_device_brain_extracellular_matrix_promotes_structural.md).
- **Supplies the disease-modeling logic and concrete ASD-adjacent exemplars** (Timothy, Angelman, TSC, idiopathic ASD E-I imbalance, oRGC convergence) plus the screening paradigm (mosaic CRISPR; patient-iPSC + isogenic controls) that the review's "programmable/proactive autism genetics" argument builds on — connecting to in-corpus screens ([Li 2023](li_2023_single_cell_brain_organoid_screening_identifies_developmental.md), [Meng 2023](meng_2023_assembloid_crispr_screens_reveal_impact_disease_genes.md)).
- **Use as orientation, not evidence.** Being a review, it should support framing and direct readers to primaries; specific quantitative ASD claims should be cited from the underlying experimental papers in this collection.

## Open questions / limitations

(Limitations the review itself emphasizes for organoid models — directly reusable as the ASD review's caveats.)

- **No functional vasculature** -> size exceeds passive O2 diffusion, causing necrotic cores and an artificial cell-stress state (per scRNA/multi-omics meta-analyses); vascularization by co-culture/induction/transplantation and sliced cultures help but lack scalability.
- **Missing non-neuronal cell types** in standard protocols — notably oligodendrocytes (and limited microglia/immune and vascular cells) — constraining myelination, neuroinflammation and BBB studies.
- **Tissue architecture/organization is reductionist.** Layer *identities* are specified but their **spatial six-layer organization is not recapitulated**; fused regions do not reproduce true inter-regional anatomy, limiting circuit/activity fidelity.
- **Variability/reproducibility** of unguided organoids; region-restricted (guided) organoids reduce variability but can only model phenotypes within the constrained region.
- **Late/end-stage disease and adult-onset features are unlikely to be reproduced;** organoids are best for *early* pathogenesis. The review stresses that conclusions require rigorous validation against in-vivo material and crosstalk with patient research, and that animal models remain necessary where mechanisms are conserved.
- As a narrative (non-systematic) review, coverage is selective and reflects the authors' (cerebral-organoid-pioneering) perspective.
