# An in vivo model of functional and vascularized human brain organoids.

## Source

- Authors: Mansour AA, Gonçalves JT, Bloyd CW, Li H, Fernandes S, Quang D, Johnston S, Parylak SL, Jin X, Gage FH (senior).
- Journal: Nature Biotechnology (2018); Vol 36(5), pp. 432-441.
- DOI: [10.1038/nbt.4127](https://doi.org/10.1038/nbt.4127)
- PMID: [29658944](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29658944/)
- Added via: manuscript_brain_organoid_v6 reference ingest

## Abstract

Differentiation of human pluripotent stem cells to small brain-like structures known as brain organoids offers an unprecedented opportunity to model human brain development and disease. To provide a vascularized and functional in vivo model of brain organoids, we established a method for transplanting human brain organoids into the adult mouse brain. Organoid grafts showed progressive neuronal differentiation and maturation, gliogenesis, integration of microglia, and growth of axons to multiple regions of the host brain. In vivo two-photon imaging demonstrated functional neuronal networks and blood vessels in the grafts. Finally, in vivo extracellular recording combined with optogenetics revealed intragraft neuronal activity and suggested graft-to-host functional synaptic connectivity. This combination of human neural organoids and an in vivo physiological environment in the animal brain may facilitate disease modeling under physiological conditions.

## Key findings

The founding demonstration that an intact human (cerebral/whole-brain) organoid grafted into adult mouse brain becomes **vascularized by host blood vessels and functionally integrated** - establishing transplantation as a route to overcome the in-vitro organoid limits of necrotic cores, arrested maturation, and absent vasculature/immune/circuit context. Grafts were GFP+ hESC-derived Lancaster-protocol cerebral organoids implanted into a cavity in the retrosplenial cortex of immunodeficient mice.

- **Survival and engraftment.** Mouse survival **91.8% beyond 180 days post-implantation (dpi)** (n = 61 mice, 10 experiments). Robust graft integration/survival at 0.5-3 months PI (**80% ± 4%, n = 28 mice**). Grafts survived to **233 dpi** (latest tested), still expressing NeuN and SOX2.
- **Vascularization by the host (the central result).** Host blood vessels began invading by **7-10 dpi** and were extensive by **14 dpi**; successful vascularization in **85.4% ± 6.4% of grafts**. Endoglin/CD31 endothelium present at 14 and 90 dpi but not at 5 dpi (vascularization occurs between **5 and 14 dpi**). **CD31+ vessels were NOT human-specific-CD31+ -> the vascular network is host (mouse)-derived, not organoid-derived.** Vascularization was essential: grafts that failed to vascularize showed little/no signal and did not survive.
- **Viability rescue (anti-necrosis).** In-vitro organoids showed widespread apoptosis by day 279 (vs day 31). Grafts showed limited/no detectable TUNEL at 14, 50, 233 dpi. Stage-matched comparisons significant: day 53 vs 14 dpi (P = 0.0001), day 102 vs 50 dpi (P = 0.0019), day 279 vs 233 dpi (P = 0.0082); day 31 vs 5 dpi not significant (before vascularization). Graft surface area dipped before vascularization then increased coincident with vessel ingrowth. => host vascularization rescues the cell death that in-vitro organoids progressively accumulate.
- **Functional perfusion imaged in vivo.** Two-photon imaging through a cranial window (after IV high-MW dextran) showed blood vessels with **active blood flow** inside grafts; GFP signal imaged to ~500 µm depth; flow visualized at 141 µm below graft surface (30 and 120 dpi).
- **Progressive neuronal differentiation.** SOX2+ VZ-like regions with radial morphology, PAX6+ radial glia, CTIP2+ deep-layer cortical-plate-like areas at 14 dpi (preserved dorso-cortical identity). NeuN+ neurons enriched and SMI312/MAP2 prominent by 50-90 dpi. In-vivo maturation outpaced culture: **NeuN+ at 50 dpi = 41.76% ± 1.82% vs 10.94% ± 1.35% in stage-matched 102-day in-vitro organoids (P < 0.0001)**; no significant difference at earlier stages.
- **Gliogenesis and host immune integration.** Human-specific GFAP+ astrocytes (S100β+) increased over time (rare before grafting); OLIG2+ oligodendrocytes at mid/late stages (but **no MBP -> no myelination detected**). Host **Iba1+ microglia (GFP-negative) infiltrated** the graft abundantly at 90 and 233 dpi (organoids lack microglia in vitro).
- **Long-distance axonal output + synapses.** GFP+ human axons by 90 dpi grew through cortex and corpus callosum to both hemispheres (ipsilateral densest), reaching amygdala, striatum, hippocampus, hypothalamus, thalamus, and rostral regions. Human-specific Synaptophysin (hSyn) puncta colocalized with PSD95 in host cortex (graft -> host synapses) and Synapsin/PSD95 co-puncta within graft.
- **Functional neuronal networks (in vivo).** AAV-jRGECO1a two-photon calcium imaging in awake mice: robust, recurrent, **synchronized spontaneous rhythmic Ca2+ transients** with all cells in a field participating in each burst; same neurons re-imaged across >3 months.
- **Electrophysiology + maturation gradient.** Multielectrode recordings: 115-dpi (late) grafts had many isolated active neurons (3-8/site) across depths and **strong cross-correlated firing within ±50 ms**, vs 50-dpi (mid) grafts with fewer neurons and little correlation -> progressive functional-network maturation. Firing increased on isoflurane removal and dropped on reintroduction (state/stimulus responsiveness).
- **Graft-to-host functional connectivity (optogenetics).** hSyn-ChR2-EYFP graft neurons fired to blue light (latency < 5 ms; 1 s constant / 50 Hz / 20 Hz; none in uninjected control). Stimulating graft while recording host produced host LFP responses (peak latency ~20 ms, followed by 20 Hz oscillation) -> functional graft-to-host synaptic input.
- **Behaviour.** Barnes maze: grafted vs ungrafted NOD-SCID mice showed no learning-rate difference, but grafted mice showed weaker spatial preference on the probe trial (attributed possibly to the implantation lesion). (Behaviour was not driven by the human cells here, unlike Revah 2022.)

## Methods

- **Organoids.** GFP+ hESC line (Lenti-CAG-GFP); cerebral organoids by the Lancaster/Knoblich protocol (embryoid body -> neuroepithelium -> Matrigel embedding -> bud outgrowth), grown **40-50 days** before implantation; only quality-passing organoids (cleared EB borders, radial neuroepithelium pre-embedding, defined buds without massive cysts, polarized VZ-like regions, dorsal-forebrain identity) selected.
- **Transplantation.** Intracerebral implantation into a cavity in the **retrosplenial cortex of adult NOD-SCID (Prkdc^scid) immunodeficient mice**. Rationale drawn from 1970s intracerebral grafting onto vascular-rich surfaces; adjacency to the brain ventricle (CSF circulation) proposed to aid survival. Notably **no mesenchymal or endothelial co-culture needed** for vascularization - the brain's vascular bed sufficed.
- **Readouts.** Whole-mount + confocal immunofluorescence (GFP, hNuclei, anti-human mitochondria, SOX2/PAX6/CTIP2/NeuN/MAP2/SMI312, hGFAP/S100β/OLIG2/MBP, Iba1, hSyn/Synapsin/PSD95, CD31/Endoglin, human-specific CD31); TUNEL apoptosis; cranial-window two-photon imaging of vasculature (dextran) and neuronal activity (AAV8-CaMKII-jRGECO1a); in-vivo multielectrode extracellular recording + cross-correlation; AAV9-ChR2-EYFP optogenetics graft and graft->host; Barnes maze behaviour.

## Relevance to the brain-organoid ASD review

- **Primary citation for the "vascularization of brain organoids" theme - specifically the vascularization-by-transplant route.** It is the proof that the necrotic-core / diffusion-limit problem of large brain organoids can be solved by host angiogenesis: vessels are recruited from the host within ~1-2 weeks, perfuse with real blood flow, and rescue the apoptosis that in-vitro organoids accumulate (e.g., widespread by day 279 in dish vs minimal at 233 dpi grafted). This frames transplantation as a maturation/viability enabler, not merely a circuit-integration trick.
- **Foundational precedent behind Revah 2022.** Mansour established intact-organoid (not dissociated-cell) brain transplantation with host vascularization, microglial infiltration, axonal outgrowth, and graft<->host functional connectivity; Revah 2022 builds on this with newborn-rat S1 targeting, behavioural read-out, and disease (Timothy syndrome) phenotyping. Useful in the review to show the methodological lineage of in-vivo maturation validation.
- **Contrast with in-vitro vascularization strategies.** Complements engineered-vascularization approaches (ETV2 induction, vascular-organoid assembly, microfluidic perfusion) by showing the host-graft alternative - relevant when the review weighs how to give ASD organoids a vasculature for longer, more faithful maturation.
- **Caveat for ASD use:** the human network here is in adult-mouse retrosplenial cortex, host-vascularized but with no demonstrated human-cell-driven behaviour and no myelination - i.e., a maturation/vascularization platform rather than a behavioural ASD assay.

## Open questions / limitations

- Whether transplantation actually accelerates functional circuit maturation **beyond** what long-term culture achieves, and which human developmental time point grafts correspond to, was left for future work.
- **No myelination** detected (OLIG2+ but MBP-negative).
- Vasculature, microglia, and (here) the surrounding circuit are all **host (mouse)-derived** - a xenogeneic chimera; human endothelial/immune identity not reconstituted.
- Specificity of axonal projections and precise synaptic mapping not resolved.
- Grafted mice showed a spatial-memory deficit on the Barnes probe (possible lesion artifact); behaviour was not attributable to human cells.
- Adult-host implantation into a surgical cavity (vs Revah's newborn intact-cortex targeting) likely limits circuit-level integration relative to early-postnatal transplantation.
