# The Cellular and Molecular Landscapes of the Developing Human Central Nervous System.

## Source

- Authors: Silbereis JC, Pochareddy S, Zhu Y, Li M, Sestan N
- Journal: Neuron (2016) — Neuron 89(2):248-268 (Jan 20, 2016)
- DOI: [10.1016/j.neuron.2015.12.008](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2015.12.008)
- PMID: [26796689](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26796689/)
- Added via: manuscript_brain_organoid_v6 reference ingest
- Document type: **Review** (Sestan lab, Yale). A reference synthesis of human CNS development — cellular organization, developmental timeline, and the transcriptional / epigenomic / regulatory landscapes of the developing human neocortex. Not an experimental study and **not an organoid paper**; it is the developmental ground truth that organoid models are measured against.

## Abstract

The human CNS follows a pattern of development typical of all mammals, but certain neurodevelopmental features are highly derived. Building the human CNS requires the precise orchestration and coordination of myriad molecular and cellular processes across a staggering array of cell types and over a long period of time. Dysregulation of these processes affects the structure and function of the CNS and can lead to neurological or psychiatric disorders. Recent technological advances and increased focus on human neurodevelopment have enabled a more comprehensive characterization of the human CNS and its development in both health and disease. The aim of this review is to highlight recent advancements in our understanding of the molecular and cellular landscapes of the developing human CNS, with focus on the cerebral neocortex, and the insights these findings provide into human neural evolution, function, and dysfunction.

## Key findings

Review/atlas with no new data. Quantities below are figures the review compiles and asserts (with the caveat that several are explicit ranges or estimates).

### The human CNS in numbers (scale, cost, and rate)
- **~86.1 billion neurons** in the adult brain + spinal cord (50-70 y males) and a roughly equal number of glia. Cerebral cortex alone ~**16.34 billion neurons** (incl. ~2.58 billion in white matter); published neocortical neuron counts vary up to ~2-fold (~14.7-32.0 billion).
- Neocortex has **~164 trillion synapses**; whole-CNS estimates range from several hundred trillion to **>1 quadrillion** depending on synapses-per-neuron assumptions (~7,200 to ~80,000 per neuron).
- ~**80% of cortical neurons are excitatory glutamatergic projection (pyramidal) neurons** (VZ/SVZ-derived); the rest are GABAergic interneurons, which in human **mostly originate from the ganglionic eminences (subpallium)** and migrate tangentially.
- Metabolic cost: brain uses **18% of the body's oxygen at rest** while being only ~2.5% of body weight.
- Cerebral white matter of young adults contains ~**149,000-176,000 km of myelinated axons**.
- **Rate of building**: ~3.86 million excitatory cortical neurons generated **per hour** during prenatal cortical neurogenesis; ~4.6 million/hr across the whole CNS over the neurogenic period; ~42.3 million synapses formed **per minute** (~700,000/sec) in late fetal/early postnatal neocortex.

### Developmental timeline (heterochrony is the headline)
- Prenatal development averages 38 weeks; embryonic period = first 56 days / 8 weeks (23 Carnegie stages). Neural folds appear ~23 pcd (CS 8); neuropores close by 29-30 pcd.
- Neurulation → 3 primary vesicles (prosencephalon, mesencephalon, rhombencephalon). Cerebral hemispheres appear ~33 pcd (CS 14); ~40-fold increase in brain weight over the fetal period.
- **Cortical neurogenesis is protracted in human**: neocortical plate neurogenesis predicted at **~11 days in mouse, ~67 days in macaque, ~143 days in human** (~48-191 pcd); first CP neurons ~50-51 pcd (CS 21); neocortical excitatory neuron generation ends ~27 pcw.
- Postnatal: newborn brain is ~26% of peak adult weight; reaches ~81-88% by age 3. Synaptic density peaks ~3-15 months (area-dependent); dendritic spine density on dorsolateral PFC pyramidal neurons peaks ~7th postnatal year, then declines (pruning) into the **third decade**. Myelination continues into the third decade in association cortex (more protracted than chimpanzee).
- **Inside-first/outside-last** radial migration builds the 6-layered cortical plate; laminar identity tracks birth order. Transient compartments emphasized: the **subplate (SP) zone**, which peaks ~31 pcw with ~3.6 billion cells (an order of magnitude more cells than a whole mouse brain), serves as a "waiting compartment" for thalamocortical afferents and is highly sensitive to perinatal injury.
- **Disorders have discrete ages of onset** (Fig. 2): intellectual disability and **autism spectrum disorder onset earliest**, with ADHD, anxiety, schizophrenia, substance abuse, and mood disorders skewing toward adolescence/early adulthood — framed as dysregulation of tightly timed developmental windows.

### Human-derived / species-divergent progenitor biology (most relevant for organoid framing)
- The expanded **outer subventricular zone (oSVZ)** and **outer/basal radial glia (oRG/bRG)** underlie human neocortical expansion; oSVZ is negligible in rodents but dominates over iSVZ in macaque/human. oRG cells are a distinct cell type (not merely displaced RG).
- Transcriptomic species comparisons of RG: **PDGFD/PDGFRB** enriched in human (not mouse) RG drives progenitor expansion (Lui et al.); the **hominin-specific gene ARHGAP11B** (partial duplication of ARHGAP11A) promotes basal-progenitor proliferation and ectopic cortical folding when overexpressed in mouse (Florio et al.); **NEUROG2** overexpression expands oRG/neurogenesis in ferret (Johnson et al.).
- Marker scheme used throughout (and shared by the Zika papers in this corpus): **PAX6/SOX2** = apical/radial-glial progenitors; **TBR2 (EOMES)** = intermediate/basal progenitors; **TBR1, CTIP2/FEZF2, SATB2** = projection-neuron subtypes; **reelin+ Cajal-Retzius** cells in the marginal zone.

### Transcriptional, epigenomic, and regulatory landscape
- ≥86% of protein-coding genes are used somewhere in CNS development; ~9/10 are differentially expressed/spliced across region and/or time. Variation is dominated by **space and time, not by sex/ethnicity/individual**; ~2/3 of global expression variance is prenatal/early-postnatal (periods 1-9), <1% across decades of adulthood.
- **Cerebellum is the most transcriptionally distinct** developing region; inter-areal neocortical differences follow an **"hourglass"** pattern — large in early/mid-fetal life, collapsing in infancy/childhood, re-expanding after adolescence — with prefrontal/perisylvian signatures prominent fetally and partly human-vs-macaque divergent.
- Disease integration: spatiotemporal transcriptome + mutation data converge ASD risk on **mid-fetal deep-layer projection neurons of prefrontal/motor-somatosensory cortex**; schizophrenia on fetal frontal cortex.
- Regulome/epigenome: ~400,000 putative enhancers genome-wide; **human-gained / human-accelerated regulatory regions** are enriched near brain genes (e.g., GPR56 alternative-promoter expansion linked to perisylvian patterning; a 15-bp E1m deletion causes perisylvian polymicrogyria). DNA methylation reconfigures fetally then slows; **non-CpG methylation (mCH)** accumulates postnatally (neurons >> glia) coincident with synaptogenesis. Somatic mosaicism / L1 retrotransposition contribute to neuronal diversity and some disease.

## Methods

**This is a literature review, not an experimental paper.** No new data, samples, or wet-lab/computational protocols are generated. It synthesizes (a) classical neuroanatomy/atlases (e.g., O'Rahilly & Muller; Bayer & Altman; Carnegie staging), (b) bulk developmental transcriptomes (BrainSpan / Human Brain Transcriptome; Kang 2011; Pletikos 2014; Miller 2014), (c) single-cell and zone-dissection RG studies (Lui 2014; Florio 2015; Johnson 2015; Pollen 2014), and (d) epigenome/regulome assays (p300/CBP, H3K4me1/H3K27ac/H3K4me3 ChIP-seq; whole-genome bisulfite; comparative enhancer maps). Developmental-timing estimates lean on the translatingtime.org algorithm (Workman et al. 2013). iPSC/organoid neural models (Lancaster 2013; Mariani 2015; Paşca 2015; Brennand 2011) are cited as emerging complements to scarce postmortem tissue, but the review does not itself perform organoid work.

## Relevance to the brain-organoid ASD review

- **Primary use = the developmental reference landscape / "ground truth"** the review's organoid work is benchmarked against. It supplies the canonical human cortical cell-type taxonomy (apical RG → oRG/bRG → IPC/TBR2 → layer-specific projection neurons + interneurons) and the marker panel used to score organoid fidelity elsewhere in this corpus.
- **Heterochrony background.** The protracted human timeline (143-day cortical neurogenesis, third-decade pruning/myelination, the giant transient subplate) is exactly the slow, derived developmental program that (i) organoids attempt to recapitulate and (ii) the ASD review invokes when arguing autism is a disorder of developmental *timing*. Pairs directly with the corpus's heterochrony/asynchrony organoid papers (Paulsen 2022; Camp 2015; Kanton 2019).
- **Defines the human-specific progenitor biology** (oSVZ/oRG expansion; PDGFD, ARHGAP11B, NOTCH2NL-adjacent themes) that motivates using *human* organoids rather than mouse — the key justification for the whole modeling program.
- **Anchors the gene×environment frame.** The review repeatedly stresses that the prolonged human developmental course leaves progenitors and transient compartments (VZ/SVZ, subplate) vulnerable to genetic *and environmental* insults over an unusually long window — the conceptual bridge to the Zika papers in this batch (Garcez 2016; Li 2016), which show an environmental insult hitting precisely these proliferative zones.
- **Disease-convergence logic.** Its mid-fetal deep-layer prefrontal-projection-neuron convergence for ASD gives the review a concrete spatiotemporal target that organoid models can try to reach and perturb.

## Open questions / limitations

- **Review, not primary data**; all numbers are compiled from others and several are explicit ranges/estimates (e.g., neocortical neuron count ~14.7-32.0 billion; synapses/neuron ~7,200-80,000; whole-CNS synapse total spanning ~hundreds of trillions to quadrillions). Treat as orders of magnitude, not precise values.
- Pre-dates the large single-cell/spatial developing-human-brain atlases and most current organoid benchmarking; cell-type taxonomy here is coarser than later corpus references (He 2024; Trevino 2021; Bhaduri 2020).
- Many human-specific mechanistic claims rest on cross-species *correlation* or mouse/ferret over-expression, not loss-of-function in human tissue.
- Postmortem-tissue dependence: limited, variable-quality samples constrain especially the histone-modification/ChIP-seq coverage the review itself flags as sparse.
- Heavy neocortex focus; cerebellum, brainstem, spinal cord, and non-neuronal/vascular/immune lineages are comparatively underdeveloped here.
