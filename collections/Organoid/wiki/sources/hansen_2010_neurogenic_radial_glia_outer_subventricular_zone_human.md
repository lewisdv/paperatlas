# Neurogenic radial glia in the outer subventricular zone of human neocortex.

## Source

- Authors: Hansen DV, Lui JH, Parker PR, Kriegstein AR
- Journal: Nature (2010)
- DOI: [10.1038/nature08845](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature08845)
- PMID: [20154730](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20154730/)
- Added via: manuscript_brain_organoid_v6 reference ingest

## Abstract

Neurons in the developing rodent cortex are generated from radial glial cells that function as neural stem cells. These epithelial cells line the cerebral ventricles and generate intermediate progenitor cells that migrate into the subventricular zone (SVZ) and proliferate to increase neuronal number. The developing human SVZ has a massively expanded outer region (OSVZ) thought to contribute to cortical size and complexity. However, OSVZ progenitor cell types and their contribution to neurogenesis are not well understood. Here we show that large numbers of radial glia-like cells and intermediate progenitor cells populate the human OSVZ. We find that OSVZ radial glia-like cells have a long basal process but, surprisingly, are non-epithelial as they lack contact with the ventricular surface. Using real-time imaging and clonal analysis, we demonstrate that these cells can undergo proliferative divisions and self-renewing asymmetric divisions to generate neuronal progenitor cells that can proliferate further. We also show that inhibition of Notch signalling in OSVZ progenitor cells induces their neuronal differentiation. The establishment of non-ventricular radial glia-like cells may have been a critical evolutionary advance underlying increased cortical size and complexity in the human brain.

## Key findings

Foundational primary-tissue paper that **defines the outer radial glia (oRG / OSVZ radial glia-like) cell** as a distinct, abundant, neurogenic progenitor of the human outer subventricular zone (OSVZ). Work uses human fetal cortex (gestational weeks GW11.5–17), not organoids; it is the human-developmental ground truth that the organoid ASD literature is benchmarked against.

- **oRG cells are radial glia-like but non-epithelial.** OSVZ cells display a long basal process but lack an apical/ventricular process (dye electroporation, GFP-retrovirus labelling, DiOlistics). They express RG markers SOX2, PAX6, nestin, GFAP and never early neuronal markers DCX or βIII-tubulin. Phospho-vimentin+ (M-phase) cells with this oRG morphology nearly always co-expressed SOX2 (267/272), PAX6 (27/27), nestin (31/31) and GFAP (20/20), but never DCX (0/27) or βIII-tubulin (0/22).
- **oRG cells do not contact the ventricle.** Centrosomes of oRG cells reside in the OSVZ (site of mitosis), whereas vRG centrosomes anchor at the ventricular surface; "en face" whole-mount showed >94% of ventricular endfeet contain centrosomes (i.e. originate from ventricular RG, not OSVZ cells). PAX6+ cells in the OSVZ: >90% co-express SOX2.
- **Abundance.** ~40% of all OSVZ progenitors are estimated to be RG-like (oRG) cells, based on phospho-vimentin+ basal-fibre counts (e.g. 145/367) and RG-marker correlation. Cells with frank oRG morphology account for only a subset of undifferentiated progenitors (65 oRG of 94 total SOX2+ TBR2- cells).
- **Mitotic somal translocation (MST).** Before cytokinesis the oRG soma moves rapidly up the basal fibre — a behaviour they named MST. Median translocation 57 µm (n = 59), usually <60 min, sometimes >100 µm. Distinct from the interkinetic nuclear migration of vRG/neuroepithelial cells (which move toward the ventricle). Nearly all divisions (all but 3 of hundreds observed) had a horizontal cleavage plane; the upper daughter inherits the basal fibre and retains oRG identity → asymmetric self-renewal.
- **Self-renewal + transit-amplifying daughters.** Sequential BrdU/EdU pulses spaced 36 h apart showed >half of oRG-morphology cells (13/21) were double-labelled (passed through S-phase twice); 6/21 single-labelled. Time-lapse captured oRG cells dividing twice and their lower (intermediate-progenitor, IP) daughters dividing again, supporting ≥2 OSVZ progenitor types that each divide multiple times. This contrasts with rodent SVZ IPs, which usually divide only once.
- **oRG cells are neurogenic for the excitatory lineage.** Dissociated OSVZ cultures (BrdU birth-dating): ~5% of βIII-tubulin+ neurons were BrdU+ (60/1,295). Of TBR2+ cells, 63/65 were BrdU+ and 25/63 also βIII-tubulin+ → excitatory neurogenesis in the OSVZ. Live imaging of oRG divisions yielded self-renewed oRG (SOX2+ TBR2-) plus committed TBR2+ or ASCL1+ daughters. No locally born GABA+ neuron was BrdU+ (0/82 in OSVZ culture) under these conditions; all ASCL1+ cells co-expressed SOX2 and/or TBR2 (shared precursor with excitatory lineage).
- **OSVZ becomes the predominant proliferative zone.** Fraction of cycling (Ki67+) progenitors in OSVZ vs VZ–ISVZ: >40% at GW13, ~60% at GW14–15.5, >75% by GW17. The VZ–ISVZ stays roughly constant through GW15.5 then diminishes. OSVZ and VZ–ISVZ have similar progenitor-type proportions but the OSVZ is far larger — a "duplicated neurogenic zone."
- **Notch maintains oRG identity.** HES1 was almost exclusively in PAX6+ cells (355/356 OSVZ HES1+ cells were PAX6+); ~80% of GFP+ PAX6+ SOX2+ oRG-morphology cells were HES1+ (45/56). HES1 never co-labelled with TBR2 or ASCL1. Notch inhibition with **10 µM DAPT** drove SOX2+ → TBR2+ conversion (increased TBR2+, decreased SOX2+ over time), demonstrating the neurogenic capacity of OSVZ progenitors and their dependence on Notch to stay undifferentiated.
- **Electrophysiology.** Input resistance: vRG 557.0 ± 190.4 MΩ vs oRG 515.4 ± 10.7 MΩ (comparable), both lower than multipolar (1,265 ± 217 MΩ) and bipolar (1,953 ± 357 MΩ) OSVZ cells. Dividing oRG cells showed small TTX-sensitive inward (Na+) currents (77.9 ± 34.2 pA), far smaller than a cortical-plate neuron (935.7 pA) — emerging neuronal-lineage properties.

## Methods

- **Tissue:** human fetal cortex from elective pregnancy-termination specimens (San Francisco General Hospital, <2 h post-procedure; UCSF IRB-approved, consented). Ages GW11.5–17, staged by foot length. n = 1 per age for cell-count series (limited usable tissue) — averaged over 3 representative fields.
- **Labelling / lineage:** focal dye electroporation (TexasRed-dextran) of vibratome slices; GFP-retrovirus and CMV-GFP-adenovirus for sparse labelling; DiOlistics (CM-DiI-coated tungsten beads) on pia vs ventricle; sequential BrdU→EdU (15 mg/ml, 36 h apart) for double-S-phase detection.
- **Imaging:** Leica TCS SP5 confocal time-lapse of slice cultures (~20–22-min intervals, up to 6 days; on-stage incubator 5% CO2 / 5% O2); 3D rendering and counting in Imaris/ImarisXT, co-localization via Matlab.
- **Markers:** SOX2, PAX6, nestin, GFAP, phospho-vimentin (M-phase cytoplasm), TBR2/EOMES (IPC/excitatory), ASCL1, DCX, βIII-tubulin (TuJ1), Ki67, HES1 (Notch effector), pericentrin (centrosome), β-catenin (endfeet), GABA.
- **Perturbation:** Notch inhibition with DAPT (10 µM) vs DMSO in slice culture; SOX2-vs-TBR2 nuclear-intensity quadrant analysis (>1,200 cells/condition).
- **Electrophysiology:** whole-cell patch clamp of GFP+ oRG and vRG cells (time-lapsed and blind acute); TTX (0.5 µM) to isolate Na+ current.

## Relevance to the brain-organoid ASD review

This is the **human-specific oRG biology basis** for the review's argument that human cortical disease must be modelled in human (not rodent) systems. oRG cells and the massively expanded OSVZ are a primate/human-enriched feature largely absent from lissencephalic rodent cortex; they are precisely the progenitor population that brain organoids are valued for recapitulating.

- Establishes the cell type (oRG) and behaviour (MST, repeated self-renewing/transit-amplifying divisions, Notch dependence) that downstream organoid papers use as a fidelity benchmark and as the locus of disease vulnerability. The review's "progenitor proliferation vulnerability stage" theme rests on oRG/IPC being the expanded, long-cycling human progenitor compartment defined here.
- Directly motivates the mTOR/oRG axis: [Andrews 2020](andrews_2020_mtor_signaling_regulates_morphology_migration_outer_radial.md) shows mTORC1 is selectively active in HOPX+ oRG and controls the oRG basal process/migration — i.e. the very basal process and MST behaviour first described in Hansen 2010.
- Grounds the human-fidelity claims of organoid platforms: [Lancaster 2013](lancaster_2013_cerebral_organoids_model_human_brain_development_microcephaly.md) and [Bershteyn 2017](bershteyn_2017_human_ipsc_derived_cerebral_organoids_model_cellular.md) are credited with reproducing OSVZ-like zones and oRG (including prolonged oRG mitosis), validating organoids as a stand-in for the tissue Hansen et al. characterized.
- Not itself an ASD model and not a Table-1 disease paper; it is the developmental-neuroscience foundation cited to justify why human progenitor-stage modelling (PTEN, TSC, ASD-convergence work) is necessary.

## Open questions / limitations

- **Origin of oRG cells unknown.** Authors infer oRG arise in the VZ and translocate out via MST, but the lineal origin was not directly demonstrated.
- **Low n / primary-tissue constraints.** Cell-count age series is n = 1 per gestational age; many quantifications are descriptive counts from limited specimens.
- **GABAergic neurogenesis unresolved.** No locally born GABA+ neuron was BrdU-labelled and all ASCL1+ cells co-expressed SOX2/TBR2, so distinct inhibitory-lineage precursors could not be defined — could be a true shared precursor or a culture-condition limitation (authors flag both).
- **Generality across species.** Whether oRG/OSVZ are a general feature of gyrencephalic (e.g. ferret, cat) vs specifically primate brains was left as a future question.
- **Functional output of two neuron origins.** Whether neurons born in the OSVZ vs VZ–ISVZ differ functionally was not addressed.
