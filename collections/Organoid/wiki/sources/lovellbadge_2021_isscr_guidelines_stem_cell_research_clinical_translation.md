# ISSCR Guidelines for Stem Cell Research and Clinical Translation: The 2021 update.

## Abstract

The International Society for Stem Cell Research has updated its Guidelines for Stem Cell Research and Clinical Translation in order to address advances in stem cell science and other relevant fields, together with the associated ethical, social, and policy issues that have arisen since the last update in 2016. While growing to encompass the evolving science, clinical applications of stem cells, and the increasingly complex implications of stem cell research for society, the basic principles underlying the Guidelines remain unchanged, and they will continue to serve as the standard for the field and as a resource for scientists, regulators, funders, physicians, and members of the public, including patients. A summary of the key updates and issues is presented here.

## Key findings

This is a **guidelines / governance document**, so "findings" are normative recommendations and category placements rather than experimental results.

### Review framework (replaces named committees with a function)
- The 2021 update no longer mandates a specific named committee (EMRO/ESCRO/SCRO). It refers instead to a **"specialized scientific and ethics oversight process"** that can sit at institutional, local, regional, or national level, must include relevant scientific + ethical expertise plus generalists and lay members, and should aim for consistency across jurisdictions.
- Research is sorted into a revised, subdivided category scheme (Box 2):
  - **Category 1A — exempt** from specialized oversight (still subject to normal lab/animal review + donor consent): most in vitro pluripotent stem cell research; **most in vitro organoid research**; transfer of human stem cells into postnatal animal hosts.
  - **Category 1B — reportable but not normally further reviewed** (new): non-integrated stem-cell-based embryo models (e.g., gastruloids); in vitro culture of human-into-non-human chimeric embryos; in vitro gametogenesis without fertilization/embryo creation.
  - **Category 2 — reviewed** by the specialized process: procurement of embryos/gametes for research; derivation of cell lines from embryos; genetic alteration of embryos/gametes; in vitro human embryo culture **up to primitive streak or 14 days (whichever first)**; integrated stem-cell-based embryo models (e.g., blastoids); human cells into non-human embryos gestated in a non-human uterus; transfer of MRT embryos to a human uterus.
  - **Category 3A — not allowed, currently unsafe** (defensible rationale exists): heritable/germline genome editing for reproduction; transferring mtDNA-edited (non-MRT) embryos to a uterus; using stem-cell-derived gametes for reproduction.
  - **Category 3B — not allowed, lacks rationale / broadly unethical**: gestating stem-cell-based embryo models; human reproductive cloning; breeding human-animal chimeras that may carry human germ cells; transferring human-animal chimeric embryos to a human or non-human-primate uterus; transferring human embryos to an animal uterus.

### Organoids (the directly relevant section)
- Methods to derive specific cell types, tissues, and organoids have expanded since 2016, but **most organoids "raise few ethical concerns."**
- Media coverage prompted explicit discussion of whether **CNS / brain organoids** warrant specialized review, including whether they could **achieve consciousness or perceive pain**. The Guidelines' stated position: **"at this time, there is no biological evidence to support such concerns,"** because consciousness/pain require complexity, maturity, and sensory-system connections **"not achieved in any current culture system."**
- Consequently **all organoid research is currently placed in Category 1A** (exempt). The document explicitly flags this as provisional: ISSCR and future committees **should revisit the topic as the science advances** (citing the 2021 National Academies report on neural organoids, transplants, and chimeras).

### Embryo culture, embryo models, chimeras, MRT, genome editing (context for the review categories)
- In vitro culture of intact human embryos **beyond 14 days / primitive streak was moved out of the prohibited Category 3 into Category 2** — i.e., it is no longer flatly banned but requires specialized review plus, as a precondition, public/national-academy dialogue before any such application is even considered (Recommendation 2.2.2.1). This reflects building pressure to relax the long-standing "14-day rule."
- **Embryo models** are now split: non-integrated (Category 1B) vs integrated models such as blastoids (Category 2); transfer of any such model to a uterus is prohibited (Category 3B), and no fixed time limit (e.g., 14 days) is applied to integrated models because they are not bona fide embryos.
- **Human-animal chimeras**: graded by recipient stage and human-cell potential; particular caution where there could be substantial human contribution to the CNS or germline (stepwise review, attention to behavior/welfare).
- **Mitochondrial replacement techniques (MRT)** moved from Category 3 to Category 2, limited to high risk of transmitting serious mtDNA disease; **not** recommended for unexplained female infertility; flags the "reversion" safety concern.
- **Somatic genome editing** basic/preclinical work is Category 1A; clinical use leans on existing gene-therapy oversight. Heritable genome editing remains prohibited (Category 3A). The update points to the WHO Expert Advisory Committee framework (then due May 2021).

### Procurement and clinical translation
- New **three-tier procurement** scheme: Tier 1 (banked/historical lines, permissible under original consent; not for reproductive use), Tier 2 (fresh somatic cells/tissues, existing committees + stem cell expertise), Tier 3 (gametes/embryos, full specialized review with monitoring against undue inducement).
- **Clinical translation** recommendations strengthened: forceful caution against premature commercialization of unproven stem cell "treatments"; classify substantially manipulated / non-homologous products as drugs/ATMPs (aligning with FDA/EMA/TGA); require substantial evidence of effectiveness from adequately powered, well-controlled trials; registries are tracking tools, **not** substitutes for RCTs; new attention to sex as a biological variable.

## Methods

Not an experimental study. Process described in Boxes 1-2:
- ISSCR Board established a **Guidelines Revision Task Force of 45 members** (the article's authors) in June 2019, plus a 10-member steering committee.
- Four working groups: (1) genome editing & MRT; (2) embryos, embryo models & gametogenesis; (3) **organoid and chimera research**; (4) regulatory, pricing & access.
- ~15 months of drafting; internal ISSCR committee + Board review; **international external peer review (Sept-Oct 2020)**; member briefings (Nov 2020); Board approval Dec 2020.
- Output is the consensus Guidelines document; this paper is the summary. Companion white papers in the same issue (e.g., Clark et al. 2021 on embryo research/embryo models/IVG; Hyun et al. 2021 on transfer into animal hosts and on research beyond the primitive streak) carry the detailed reasoning.

## Relevance to the brain-organoid ASD review

- **Primary use = the ethics / governance citation** for the review. This is the canonical, current (2021) field-consensus statement that **brain-organoid research sits in the exempt Category 1A** and that, as of writing, there is **no biological evidence** brain organoids achieve consciousness or pain perception.
- Directly supports the review's "organoid research ETHICS/guidelines" theme and underwrites the legitimacy of running large genetic-perturbation and functional-readout programs in brain organoids (e.g., the CRISPR-screen and network-activity papers in this corpus) without specialized oversight, while donor consent still applies.
- Provides the caveat the review should carry: the Category 1A placement is **explicitly provisional and to be revisited** as organoids gain maturity, sensory connectivity, or are combined with chimera/transplant approaches — relevant when arguing for increasingly "programmable" or functionally mature organoids.
- Useful boundary-setter: the same framework that exempts organoids draws hard lines (Category 3A/3B) around heritable editing and gestating embryo models, so a "proactive autism genetics" agenda framed around organoid models stays well inside permitted research, whereas any germline-editing extension would not.

## Open questions / limitations

- **Provisional and qualitative.** The consciousness/pain position is asserted as "no biological evidence" rather than operationalized; the Guidelines set no threshold (maturity, network complexity, sensory integration) that would trigger reclassification out of Category 1A.
- This is a summary; exact recommendation wording and numbering require the full ISSCR Guidelines / supplemental documents, which are not in this PDF.
- Guidelines are **voluntary professional standards**, not law; national legislation varies and may be more or less permissive (e.g., the 14-day rule is statutory in some countries).
- Several relaxations (14-day rule into Category 2, MRT into Category 2) are explicitly contingent on public engagement and regulator buy-in that may not exist in a given jurisdiction.
- Strong overlap with the companion brain-organoid ethics review (Hyun 2020) on the same themes (oversight, consent, consciousness/moral status); the two should be read together rather than double-counted.
